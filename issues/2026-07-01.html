Longer, not larger

Two ideas landed at almost the same moment this week: Anthropic's best affordable model went live as the default for millions of users, and a Chinese research lab quietly published a paper that reframes the whole game — showing that a smaller model can match much larger rivals if you simply let it think in far longer chains of action.

The one thing

Anthropic launched Claude Sonnet 5 on June 30, making it the new default for anyone on the free and pro plans at claude.ai. The headline is what it can actually do now: plan across many steps, use tools like web browsers and code terminals, and carry complex tasks through to completion — tasks that previous mid-tier models would routinely abandon midway. Anthropic says its agentic performance now approaches that of Opus 4.8, its flagship. The introductory price is $2 per million input tokens and $10 per million output tokens through August 31, after which it rises to $3 and $15. For companies running many automated tasks at scale, that gap in capability for a fraction of the cost is the whole story.

Paper, explained: thinking in longer chains

Scaling the Horizon, Not the Parameters: Reaching Trillion-Parameter Performance with a 35B Agent · InternScience / Shanghai AI Laboratory · arXiv 2606.30616

A 35-billion-parameter model — mid-sized by today's standards — scored at the level of trillion-parameter systems on several demanding benchmarks. The trick wasn't a bigger brain. It was a much longer look.

The problem, and why it's hard

For the past few years the dominant recipe for making AI more capable has been simple: make it bigger. Add more parameters — the numerical weights inside the model that encode everything it knows — and performance goes up. This works, but the cost compounds fast. A trillion-parameter model is enormously expensive to train and to run, putting it out of reach for most organizations. The question Shanghai AI Laboratory's InternScience team asked was whether there is another dimension to scale along — one that doesn't require a bigger model.

Making a smaller model truly competitive with the giants is genuinely difficult. Smaller models know less, have less working memory, and get confused faster when tasks stretch across many steps. You can throw training tricks at the problem, but you run into the same wall: there is only so much knowledge you can pack into 35 billion parameters, compared to a trillion.

How it works

The key insight: instead of scaling the model, scale the horizon — the length of the reasoning chain the model works through before arriving at an answer. Think of it like chess. One way to become a stronger player is to study more: memorize more openings, internalize more positions, build a bigger mental library. Another way is to think further ahead — to trace out longer sequences of possible moves before picking one, even if your raw knowledge base stays the same. The InternScience team chose the second path.

Their model, Agents-A1, is what researchers call an "agent": rather than answering a question in one shot, it takes a sequence of actions — searching a knowledge source, reading the result, deciding what to do next, taking another action, observing what happened, and checking that observation against a verifier — a separate system that scores whether the action was any good. The length of that sequence is the "horizon." Most AI systems today work with short horizons; Agents-A1 was trained to work through very long ones, averaging 45,000 tokens per task. For reference, 45,000 tokens is roughly the length of a short novel.

Training happened in three stages. First, the team ran full-domain fine-tuning across a broad range of agentic tasks, teaching the base model to behave like an agent at all. Then they trained six separate specialist "teacher" models — each highly competent in one domain: web search, scientific reasoning, software engineering, instruction following, molecular biology, and so on. Finally, in the cleverest step, they used a technique called "multi-teacher domain-routed on-policy distillation" to fold all six specialists back into one student model. In plain English: each time the student model worked through a problem, the relevant teacher checked its reasoning and gave feedback, and the student updated accordingly — simultaneously across all six domains — until it absorbed the specialist knowledge without needing to be six separate models at runtime.

What they actually did

The team trained Agents-A1 as a 35-billion-parameter Mixture-of-Experts model — an architecture where only a subset of parameters activates for any given task, making it cheaper to run than a dense model of the same nominal size. They then benchmarked it against trillion-parameter systems including Kimi K2.6 and DeepSeek V4-Pro, as well as GPT-5.5, across tasks spanning long-horizon web search, instruction following, frontier scientific reasoning, software engineering, and molecular biology.

What they found

On SEAL-0, a challenging long-horizon search benchmark, Agents-A1 scored 56.4 — competitive with the trillion-parameter models. On IFBench (instruction following) it hit 80.6; on FrontierScience-Olympiad (scientific reasoning) it reached 79.0; on GAIA, a hard general-agent benchmark, it hit 96.0. It remained highly competitive — though not uniformly top — on BrowseComp (75.5), SciCode (44.3), and the Humanity's Last Exam benchmark (47.6). Across the suite, the model consistently punched above its weight class.

The catch

Competitive is not the same as universally better. Agents-A1 doesn't beat trillion-parameter models on every task — it matches them on the specific tasks where long-horizon reasoning is the bottleneck. There is also a practical cost: a 45,000-token trajectory takes much more compute per task than a quick single-shot query. You save on the model's weight; you pay in inference time and latency. For deliberate research or analysis tasks this trade-off makes sense. For anything requiring a fast response, it doesn't.

If you remember one thing: Making a model bigger is not the only way to make it smarter — training it to reason in much longer chains of action can get you to near-trillion-parameter performance at a fraction of the model size.

Why it matters

Two ideas converged this week, and they point in the same direction. Anthropic made near-flagship capability affordable to anyone with a free account. Simultaneously, a research lab in Shanghai demonstrated that the efficiency frontier is still moving fast — you can reach near-trillion-parameter results with a 35-billion-parameter model, as long as you engineer how it reasons rather than simply how large it is.

Together, these move the central question from "how big can we make the model?" to "how cleverly can we use what we have?" That shift has real consequences. It means better performance doesn't automatically require more energy, more hardware, or more money. It might just require better training design and longer chains of thought — two things that don't scale with raw compute the way parameters do.

Decoder

Word of the day
Horizon scaling
Training an AI to complete tasks by working through much longer chains of action — think, act, observe, repeat — rather than answering in one shot. Like teaching a chess player to think twenty moves ahead rather than giving them a bigger brain to memorize more positions.
Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)
A model design where only a fraction of the total parameters activate for any given input. A 35B MoE model is cheaper to run than a 35B dense model doing the same thing — most of the weights sit idle.
Agentic
An AI that takes a sequence of actions — searching, writing code, clicking, deciding — rather than producing one answer. Claude Sonnet 5's main selling point this week; also what Agents-A1 is built to do.
Distillation
Teaching a small model to mimic a larger one by having it learn from the bigger model's outputs rather than raw data alone. Agents-A1 uses six specialist "teacher" models distilled into one student.
Trajectory
The full recorded sequence of an AI agent's actions and observations during a task — every think, act, and observe step end to end. Agents-A1's training trajectories average 45,000 tokens.
Benchmark
A standardized test used to compare AI models fairly. Results are only as meaningful as how well the test reflects real-world use — a common caveat worth keeping in mind.
