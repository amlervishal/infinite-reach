Signals from Shanghai

For the first time in eight years of the event, China's president will deliver the keynote at the country's flagship technology summit — a signal about who is taking the governance of intelligence seriously. Meanwhile, a lab in Shenzhen demonstrates how to diagnose cancer on a loop of optical fiber, and Europe writes its largest-ever check for an AI defense company.

The one thing

Xi Jinping will appear at the 2026 World AI Conference in Shanghai, which opens on Thursday July 17, and deliver a keynote address — his first in the eight-year history of the annual summit. The signal is deliberate. China has been accelerating the establishment of a proposed World AI Cooperation Organization (WAICO), which it wants headquartered in Shanghai, as a counterweight to Western-led governance bodies. Analysts expect Xi to use the keynote to give WAICO definition and momentum. The conference will feature more than 140 forums, 1,400 guests, and 300 global product debuts, with global AI governance as the central theme. The US-China technology rivalry has intensified sharply in 2026, but China's move here is not primarily about competition with a single company — it's about setting the rules of the road before anyone else does, at a global scale.

Paper, explained: a neural network that runs on light

"Tunable phosphorene modulators – accelerating medical diagnosis with ultra-efficient photonic platforms" · Han Zhang et al., Shenzhen University · Opto-Electronic Advances, May 2026

A team at Shenzhen University published a paper this spring that does something quietly remarkable: it runs a complete, working neural network entirely on light, tests it on real clinical images, and matches the diagnostic accuracy of a senior physician — while consuming 246 times less energy than a conventional computer doing the same job. It's been making rounds quietly since May. Here's why it deserves a slower read.

The problem, and why it's hard

Every hospital screening programme faces the same bottleneck: not enough specialists, too many scans. Software that can reliably flag a suspicious retinal image or a liver lesion before a doctor reviews it would save time, catch disease earlier, and extend quality care to clinics that can't afford a full imaging team. The problem has always been energy and cost. The kind of neural network accurate enough to do this job runs on powerful graphics processors — GPUs — that consume large amounts of electricity, generate significant heat, and are expensive to deploy in resource-limited settings. For years, researchers treated this as a chip-design problem: build faster silicon, reduce the wattage. The Shenzhen team asked a different question: what if you skip the electrons entirely?

How it works

The analogy to carry through this section is a pipe organ versus a piano. A piano makes sound by hammers striking strings — one physical impact per key, effort concentrated at each individual press. A pipe organ sends air through tubes; the same sustained breath activates multiple notes simultaneously, and the energy spreads across the system rather than concentrating at each key. Conventional computing is the piano: a GPU performs neural network inference by switching millions of transistors on and off in sequence, each switch consuming a tiny burst of power and generating heat. A photonic neural network is the organ: information travels as light through glass fiber, and the computation happens as light interacts with specially engineered materials along the path — no mechanical switching, no transistor heat.

The key material in this paper is phosphorene: a single-atom-thick sheet of black phosphorus, related to graphite but with a crucial property — how it interacts with light can be tuned by applying a small voltage. The team wrapped this phosphorene around a tiny loop of optical fiber called a microring resonator, which greatly amplifies how strongly the material and the light interact. The result is an electro-optic modulator with a modulation efficiency of 0.25 V·cm — technically a low value, meaning very little electrical energy is needed to change the light signal. Think of it as a highly responsive valve: a small turn of the handle diverts a large flow of water. Apply a voltage; the light changes; that change carries data.

Using several of these modulators in a technique called time-division multiplexing — where one fiber takes turns carrying different parts of the calculation in rapid alternating sequence, like several cars sharing a single lane — the researchers assembled a full photonic neural network (PNN) from standard off-the-shelf fiber components. No exotic fabrication facility, no cleanroom. Just glass, phosphorene, and carefully arranged light.

What they actually did

The team didn't stop at characterising the modulator in isolation. They assembled the complete system — from input image data through the photonic layers to final classification — and ran it against two clinical tasks with real consequences: detecting retinal detachment in fundus photographs (standard images taken of the inside of the eye), and identifying liver cancer in abdominal scans. Both conditions are ones where delayed diagnosis carries severe costs: untreated retinal detachment can cause permanent blindness within days; liver cancer is frequently caught only after it has advanced. The system received image data, processed it through its fiber-optic layers, and produced a diagnosis. The researchers measured accuracy against expert radiologist classifications, and energy consumption against an equivalent GPU-based inference run.

What they found

Diagnostic accuracy matched that of senior radiologists on both tasks. The headline figure is the energy result: 246 times more efficient than the GPU baseline. The system produced correct classifications throughout the test sets, without major failure categories on well-formed inputs. The authors flag one key constraint the prototype currently carries: it operates on images at a fixed resolution, through a specific preprocessing pipeline. A raw scan from an arbitrary hospital machine can't be fed directly — it needs to pass through that pipeline first, which limits plug-and-play deployment to settings where that preprocessing can be standardised.

The catch

This is a proof of concept, not a clinical device. The test datasets are not described with the granularity needed to assess generalisability: demographic diversity, image-machine variety, and edge-case presentations (unusual anatomy, low image quality) are not reported. The 246× efficiency comparison is specifically against a general-purpose GPU; a purpose-built electronic inference chip (the kind used in commercial AI products) would land somewhere between the two. And photonic systems are temperature-sensitive in a way silicon chips are not — keeping the modulator's calibration stable across the temperature swings of a real hospital environment is an engineering challenge the paper defers to future work.

If you remember one thing: A lab built a neural network out of glass fiber and a single-atom-thick sheet of black phosphorus that diagnosed cancer and retinal detachment as well as an expert physician, at 1/246th the electricity — a genuine demonstration that the hardware we use to run intelligence doesn't have to be silicon.

Why it matters

Three threads from this week, taken together, point at the same underlying shift. China is moving to own the governance conversation around intelligence — not just the technology, but the rules, the institutions, and the international framing. Europe is pouring its largest-ever defense check into a company that uses software to control drones and battlefield operations. And a university lab in Shenzhen is quietly demonstrating that the silicon monopoly on AI inference has a challenger: glass and light. The common thread is that AI is no longer primarily a software competition. The race has moved into physical materials, geopolitical architecture, and institutional frameworks. The team that writes the best code matters less than the team that controls what the code runs on — and where the world agrees it should operate.

Decoder

Word of the day
Phosphorene
A single-atom-thick sheet of black phosphorus — imagine peeling one layer off a block of graphite pencil, except it's phosphorus atoms instead of carbon. That one-atom layer has an unusual property: how it interacts with light can be controlled by applying a small voltage, like a dimmer switch for a beam of light. This makes it ideal for building the "valves" inside a fiber-optic neural network. The Shenzhen team used it as the tunable heart of their system.
Photonic neural network (PNN)
A neural network where the math is performed by light pulses traveling through glass fiber rather than electrons through silicon. Faster and cooler to run, but more sensitive to temperature changes and harder to scale up today.
Electro-optic modulator
A device that converts an electrical signal into a change in a beam of light — think of it as a light-volume knob controlled by electricity. In the Shenzhen system, the phosphorene layer makes this modulator highly efficient: small voltage, large effect.
Time-division multiplexing
Sending multiple signals over one fiber by having each signal take turns in rapid sequence — like several car lanes merging into one, with each car taking an assigned slot. The Shenzhen team used this to fit a larger neural network into a simpler physical setup.
Loop engineering
A workflow pattern where, instead of a human typing prompts to an AI, the human writes a program that automatically generates prompts, evaluates outputs, and decides what to ask next — running unsupervised in a continuous cycle. The engineer designs the loop; the model does the work inside it.
WAICO
World AI Cooperation Organization — a China-proposed international body that would govern AI development globally, which Beijing wants headquartered in Shanghai. Think of it as China's bid to create a UN-like institution for AI, before Western nations establish their own framework instead.
