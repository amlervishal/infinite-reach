Paper, explained: How agents change the shape of work

How AI Agents Reshape Knowledge Work: Autonomy, Efficiency, and Scope · Jeremy Yang (Harvard Business School), Kate Zyskowski, Noah Yonack, Jerry Ma (Perplexity AI) · arXiv:2606.07489

Nearly every AI lab claims its agents are transforming how people work. This paper actually checked — with real production data from millions of sessions — and found that autonomous agents shift not just how fast work gets done, but what work people attempt at all.

The problem, and why it's hard

We have good intuitions about what AI assistants are useful for: they answer questions faster than a search engine, summarise documents, draft emails. But agents — systems that can autonomously plan and execute multi-step tasks without someone guiding every move — are supposed to be different in kind, not just degree. The question is whether that's really true, or whether it's marketing.

Proving it is harder than it sounds. If you show that agent users get better results, you can't rule out that agent users are simply more sophisticated people with more complex needs. They self-selected into a different product. To isolate the tool's contribution, you need to compare users who asked similar questions but got different tools — and you need real work, not an experimental prompt designed by a researcher. The team at Harvard and Perplexity had access to exactly that.

How it works — and the analogy that unlocks it

Picture the difference between a taxi and a moving company. A taxi takes you somewhere quickly; a moving company handles an entire relocation while you do something else entirely. Both are "transportation," but they're designed for different jobs at different scales of complexity.

Perplexity Search is the taxi: you ask a specific question, get a cited answer in seconds, and decide what to do next. Perplexity Computer is the moving company: you describe a goal — "research the three best cloud providers for a startup, compare pricing, and draft a recommendation memo" — and the system decomposes that into subtasks, routes each one to whichever of its 19 specialised models is best suited (one for browsing, one for writing, one for data analysis, and so on), and hands you a finished result. You come back later.

The paper builds a precise framework around this taxi-versus-moving-company distinction. Every task has a fixed delegation cost — the overhead of handing it off: explaining the goal clearly enough for the agent to act unsupervised, reviewing its output. Agents are slower to start than a quick search. But once running, agents have a much lower marginal execution cost per step: each additional sub-task the agent completes costs you almost nothing in attention, because the system handles it. That means agents only make sense above some threshold of task complexity. For a quick factual question, Search wins. For a 40-step research project, the overhead of Search (you'd have to orchestrate each search yourself, synthesise the results, decide what to look up next) becomes the bottleneck. The agent removes that bottleneck.

This shifts what the researchers call the affordable task frontier — the set of tasks a person will realistically attempt given their time and attention. With an agent, that frontier expands upward into territory that was previously too costly to enter.

What they actually did

The team matched near-identical initial queries between Perplexity Search (available on the $20/month Pro plan) and Perplexity Computer (available on the $200/month Max plan). Using matched pairs controls for the content of the starting question; any difference in what happens next reflects the tool, not the question. They measured three things: how much autonomous work the system performed per session, what types of follow-up queries users sent after the initial result, and how often users expressed dissatisfaction with responses.

What they found

Three numbers define the paper. First: Computer performed 26 minutes of autonomous work per session, compared to 33 seconds for Search on near-identical starting queries. That's not a modest speedup — it's a change in kind. Second: users of Computer shifted their follow-up activity toward verification and extension — reviewing the agent's work, asking it to go deeper — rather than the first-answer queries that dominate Search sessions. They were acting as editors reviewing a draft, not searchers hunting for an answer. Third: per-query dissatisfaction rates were 55% lower on Computer than Search. Users were getting better results on harder tasks.

The task-type shift is the conceptually important finding. It suggests that agents pull users toward more ambitious tasks — not because users became more ambitious, but because the agent absorbed the overhead that previously made those tasks impractical to start.

The catch

This is one company, two products, at one price point. The two user bases may differ in ways the query-matching approach cannot fully control: someone paying $200/month is probably a more sophisticated user to begin with. The paper is honest about this but cannot eliminate the selection effect entirely. More importantly, the paper measures satisfaction, not outcomes — users were less likely to express frustration with Computer, but whether agents produced better decisions, better work, or better business results remains unmeasured. The expanded task frontier may produce more ambitious failures as well as more ambitious successes.

If you remember one thing: Agents don't just do the same work faster — they shift what work you attempt in the first place, expanding the ceiling of what a single person considers worth starting.

Why it matters

Two things happened today that, read together, describe the same underlying shift. Apple — the most hardware-centric, vertically-integrated company in consumer technology — licensed the intelligence layer of its flagship product from a competitor, because building frontier AI from scratch turned out to be harder than building iPhones. The $1 billion a year it's paying Google is a measure of how much the balance of power has shifted in one decade. And the Perplexity paper showed, with real data, that autonomous agents are already changing the ceiling of what individuals attempt: not slightly more, but tasks that wouldn't have been started at all. The question is no longer whether this technology changes how work gets done. The question is whether the people, institutions, and companies deploying these tools will expand their ambitions as fast as the tools are expanding.

Decoder

Word of the day
Extensions (iOS 27)
Apple's new system that lets you swap out the AI model powering Siri for any third-party one — Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or others. Think of it as a socket in iOS where the default occupant (Siri's cloud model) can be replaced. What's unusual is that Apple, which famously controls every pixel of the iPhone experience, decided to make this swap possible at all.
Mixture of Experts (MoE)
A model architecture where only a fraction of the total parameters activates for any given query. Gemini's 1.2-trillion-parameter model does not run all 1.2 trillion parameters at once — it routes each input to a specialist subset, which is how a model that large can be fast enough for a real-time assistant.
SWE-bench
A benchmark where an AI is given real, unresolved GitHub issues from popular open-source software and must fix the code without hints. It is harder than most coding tests because the problems are genuine, not designed to be tractable.
Affordable task frontier
From the Perplexity paper: the set of tasks a person will realistically attempt given their time and attention budget. The paper's key claim is that agents push this frontier outward — making it worthwhile to start tasks that previously required too much manual overhead.
Agentic
Describes an AI system that takes a sequence of actions on its own — browsing, writing code, calling external tools — rather than just producing one response and stopping. Perplexity Computer is agentic; Perplexity Search is not.
Delegation cost
The overhead of handing a task to an agent: articulating the goal precisely enough for it to act unsupervised, then reviewing its output. This fixed cost is why agents make most sense for complex, multi-step work rather than quick questions — the delegation cost becomes a smaller fraction of total effort the more steps the agent handles.
