When software started managing itself

A new model that runs a hundred helpers at once, Google quietly rewiring how the whole planet searches, and a word that went from niche to everywhere in a week: harness. Here's the week, in plain English.

The one thing

Yesterday Anthropic released Opus 4.8, its most capable model so far — just 41 days after the previous one. The headline isn't that it's smarter at answering questions. It's a feature called Dynamic Workflows: the model can now spin up hundreds of copies of itself to work on a big job in parallel, like a manager handing pieces of a project to a hundred assistants and stitching their work back together. In a demo, this setup rewrote an entire large software project from start to finish on its own. We've quietly crossed from "AI that answers" to "AI that runs a whole job."

  • SearchOn May 26, Google switched its core search engine over to a model called Gemini 3.5 Flash. The thing billions of people use every day is now answered by a language model under the hood — a big bet, running live.

Deep dive: what people mean by a "harness"

You'll hear this word constantly now, so let's demystify it. A raw language model is just a thing that predicts text. On its own it can't browse the web, run code, remember yesterday, or check its own work. A harness is the scaffolding built around the model that gives it those powers — tools to use, a memory, and rules for what to do step by step.

The analogy: the model is the engine; the harness is the rest of the car — wheels, steering, dashboard. A brilliant engine sitting on the floor doesn't take you anywhere. Lately, a lot of the visible progress in AI isn't a smarter engine at all — it's a better car built around a similar engine. That's why the same underlying model can feel dramatically more capable inside a good harness.

When you combine a strong model, a good harness, and the ability to run many copies at once — that's what people now call agentic: software that takes a goal and works toward it across many steps, instead of waiting for you to prompt it each time. Opus 4.8's Dynamic Workflows is exactly this, turned up to a hundred helpers.

Paper, explained: can an AI bluff its way through a deal?

“Cattle Trade: A Multi-Agent Benchmark for LLM Bluffing, Bidding, and Bargaining” · Robert & Clemens Müller · arXiv

You'll never read an AI research paper — so each issue we'll read one for you and explain it plainly. This week's is genuinely fun. We usually test AI one skill at a time: can it answer a question, write some code, solve a puzzle? But real life mixes skills together. In a negotiation you bid, bluff, size up the other person, and watch your budget — all at once. These researchers wanted to know: can today's AI models juggle all of that, against other AIs, when nobody can see everyone's cards?

So they built a game — think a long round of Monopoly crossed with poker. Over 50–60 turns the players run auctions, make secret offers, haggle, bluff, and manage a limited pot of money. They dropped seven of the cheaper AI models — plus three simple hand-coded rule-following bots — into 242 games and watched how each one played.

The results are humbling for the robots. The winners weren't the ones who spent the most — they were the disciplined ones: spending efficiently, not blowing the budget, and adjusting as the game shifted. Strikingly, the simple rule-following bots beat most of the AI models. And the AIs failed in very human ways — overpaying, accidentally bidding against themselves, going broke by starting deals they couldn't afford, and not adapting to what opponents were doing.

Why it's worth knowing: we're about to hand AI real tasks that involve money and other people — booking, negotiating, managing resources. This is a reminder that being clever at answering questions isn't the same as being street-smart in a competition. For now, careful and disciplined beats flashy — and plain old rules can still outplay a big model.

Word of the day
Harness
The scaffolding built around an AI model — its tools, memory, and step-by-step rules. The model is the engine; the harness is the rest of the car.
Agentic
Software that takes a goal and works toward it over many steps on its own, instead of waiting for a new instruction each time.
Subagent
A copy of the model handed one small piece of a bigger job, working in parallel with other copies.
Frontier model
One of the largest, most capable AI systems at the cutting edge — e.g. Opus, Gemini, GPT.
Benchmark
A standard test used to measure and compare how good AI models are at some skill.
IPO
"Initial public offering" — when a private company first sells shares to the public on a stock market.
