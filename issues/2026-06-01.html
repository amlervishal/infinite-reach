One move at a time

Paper, explained: do language models actually plan ahead?

Extracting Search Trees from LLM Reasoning Traces Reveals Myopic Planning · Sixing Chen, Ji-An Li, Saner Cakir, Sinan Akcali, Kayla Lee, Marcelo G. Mattar · arXiv 2605.06840

When a reasoning model works through a hard problem, its output can look genuinely sophisticated — branching into possibilities, rejecting some, returning to others. This paper builds a method to test whether that appearance maps onto reality, using a board game where "planning" is easy to measure.

The problem, and why it's hard

Modern language models with "chain-of-thought" reasoning often produce thousands of words of visible deliberation. Whether that deliberation actually drives their decisions is genuinely hard to know. A chess engine has a measurable search depth you can inspect directly. An LLM's reasoning trace is natural language; you can't simply read off "this model evaluated four moves ahead" or check whether any of that deep reasoning changed the outcome.

This matters beyond the academic: if deep reasoning in the traces doesn't influence decisions, then the apparent sophistication of a model's planning is mostly theater. And you'd want to know that before deploying it on tasks where genuine foresight is the point.

How it works

The researchers built a technique to extract the implicit search tree from a model's reasoning trace. Think of it like a detective reading a transcript of someone narrating a game out loud. From the structure of their language — "if I play here, then they might respond there, so then I could..." — you can reconstruct which game states they visited, in what order, and how deep the chain went before they made a move.

They used the four-in-a-row board game (a simpler cousin of Connect Four) as their testbed, precisely because every valid game state can be enumerated in advance. That makes it possible to automatically match phrases in a model's reasoning trace to specific board states and reconstruct the full search tree the model traversed. The tree then has two measurable properties: breadth (how many options were considered at each step) and depth (how many moves ahead was the reasoning extended).

The key analytical step was fitting two competing computational models to those extracted trees. The first assumed the LLM was genuinely planning: using information from all explored branches, including deep ones, when choosing its move. The second — the "myopic" model — assumed the LLM was effectively acting on only the shallowest one or two steps and ignoring whatever else appeared further down the traces. The question: which model predicts the LLM's actual move choices better?

They also ran a causal intervention: take the full chain-of-thought traces, selectively prune the paragraphs that correspond to deep-node reasoning (the parts where the model appears to be thinking three or four moves ahead), and check whether removing those paragraphs changes the model's final move.

What they actually did

The team tested multiple LLMs — including recent reasoning models — on a set of four-in-a-row board positions. They collected human player data on the same positions for direct comparison. For each model and each human, they extracted the search tree, measured its depth and breadth, fitted both planning models, and ran the pruning experiment. The full analysis covered enough positions and players to distinguish between the two computational accounts statistically.

What they found

Two results stand out. First, LLMs' search trees were shallower than human players' trees, and model performance tracked breadth — how many different moves were considered at each level — rather than depth. Models that thought more broadly tended to play better moves; models that reasoned further ahead didn't improve correspondingly.

Second, and more striking: when the researchers pruned the deep-node paragraphs from the reasoning traces, the models' move choices barely changed. The myopic model — the one that ignores everything past the first couple of levels — predicted the LLMs' actual moves significantly better than the full-planning model. Deep exploration is happening in the traces, but it isn't driving decisions.

For human players, the pattern was the reverse. Performance was driven primarily by depth of search: the deeper a human reasoned before choosing, the better the move tended to be. Breadth barely predicted human quality at all.

The catch

This study uses one game, with a specific structure: discrete legal moves, finite enumerable states, unambiguous wins and losses. Results here may not transfer to open-ended tasks — writing, coding, diagnosis — where the "search space" isn't neatly enumerable and depth may matter differently. The paper also leaves open the question of why deep reasoning appears in the traces at all if it isn't influencing decisions. The most natural hypothesis is that it's an artifact of training: models were rewarded for producing outputs that look like careful thinking, whether or not that thinking shaped the answer.

If you remember one thing: language models write about looking far ahead, but appear to choose based on what looks good right now — at least in the domain where that claim was testable.

Why it matters

This week's two biggest stories pull in opposite directions. The myopic-planning paper reveals a gap between how language models appear to reason and how they actually decide: they write about looking far ahead but choose based on what's in front of them, at least in the setting where this was measurable. The Cognition raise is the market's answer to that finding: it doesn't matter. A $26 billion valuation and $492 million in annual revenue is an argument that shallow-but-fast pattern matching is already good enough to take over large chunks of software development. Both things can be true at once — tools that don't plan deeply can be extraordinarily useful. The question worth keeping in mind is which tasks actually require the kind of genuine foresight these systems may not yet have.

Decoder

Word of the day
Myopic planning
Myopic means nearsighted — seeing clearly only what's close. In decision-making, myopic planning means picking the option that looks best right now, without considering how things might unfold two or three moves later. Like taking a pawn in chess because it's free today, without noticing it opens your king to a checkmate next turn.
Chain-of-thought (CoT)
A technique where you ask a language model to write out its reasoning before giving a final answer — like asking a student to show their working. Makes the model's process visible, though (as this paper shows) visible isn't the same as causally influential.
Search tree
A branching diagram of possible futures in a decision problem. Each node is a state, each branch is an action. Depth = how many moves ahead you look; breadth = how many options you consider at each level.
SWE-bench Verified
A benchmark where AI models are given real bug reports from open-source software projects and scored on whether their code actually fixes the bug. Opus 4.8 scored 88.6% — up from around 2% when the benchmark launched in 2023.
AI Credits
GitHub's new metering unit for Copilot. One credit = $0.01 USD. Every conversation with a model burns credits from your monthly budget; code completions remain free. A shift from a flat-fee gym membership to a pay-per-visit model.
Valuation
The agreed price of a company at a funding round — not a market price. A $26 billion valuation means investors paid as if the whole company is worth $26B. Until an IPO or acquisition, it's a bet, not a fact.
