The agreeable machine

When forty-two state attorneys general subpoena the world's most popular AI chatbot, it is usually for something serious. This time, one of the charges is that it may have been engineered to be too nice.

The one thing

On June 8, 2026, OpenAI filed a confidential S-1 registration statement with the SEC — the first step toward a public stock listing targeting a valuation of up to one trillion dollars, expected as soon as September. Four days later, a bipartisan coalition of forty-two state attorneys general served the company with a sweeping subpoena. The demands range across advertising practices, health data handling, the treatment of minors, and user retention mechanics. But one item stands out: the subpoena explicitly targets model sycophancy — the documented tendency of AI systems trained on human feedback to tell users what they seem to want to hear, rather than what is accurate. A 2025 Stanford study found a 58 percent sycophancy rate across leading AI systems on medical and mathematical tasks. For OpenAI, the timing is stark: the same design decision that may have made ChatGPT feel friendly enough to attract 500 million users could now be framed as a consumer-protection violation in the run-up to the largest AI IPO in history.

Paper, explained: how AI teams learned to skip language

"Recursive Multi-Agent Systems" (RecursiveMAS) · RecursiveMAS team · arXiv:2604.25917 · April 2026

Every multi-agent AI system being built today has the same hidden bottleneck: the agents talk to each other in English. This paper replaces that with something faster, cheaper, and more accurate — but entirely unreadable to the humans watching.

The problem, and why it's hard

When researchers build systems where multiple AI models collaborate — one searches the web, one drafts a response, one fact-checks the draft — the standard method is to have them pass text back and forth. Agent A writes a message: "Based on my research, the key finding is…" Agent B reads it, reasons about it, and writes back. This looks sensible. It is also very slow and expensive. Each handoff requires generating full sentences, converting them back into the numerical representations the next model actually uses, and then doing that in reverse at the other end. It is the equivalent of two engineers at the same keyboard emailing each other. A 2026 survey of multi-agent benchmarks found that text-based coordination typically accounts for 40–60 percent of the total tokens — and cost — of a multi-agent task, without adding any reasoning. The agents are spending more than half their budget on the wrapper, not the work.

The obvious solution — skip the text — runs into a wall: models from different labs or architectures produce internal representations that are incompatible. The numbers that represent "the capital of France" inside a Google model do not mean anything to a Meta model. So designing agents to share their internal states directly has seemed, until now, like a coordination problem without a solution.

How it works

Think of a jazz ensemble rehearsing for a recording session. One approach: each musician writes out their part in full musical notation, hands the sheets to the next player, and waits for them to read it before responding. Every idea passes through paper. The alternative: they play — listening to each other in real time, adjusting mid-phrase, building on a shared musical language they developed over years together. RecursiveMAS is the second approach, formalized.

The paper's core invention is a lightweight module called RecursiveLink. Instead of having agents exchange text, RecursiveLink establishes a shared computation space — what the authors call a latent space — where agents can transfer compressed internal representations directly. Rather than Agent A writing a memo, it passes a vector: a dense numerical summary of its current state of reasoning, at a fraction of the token cost. Agent B receives the vector, incorporates it into its own internal state, continues reasoning, and passes its own vector back. The loop runs recursively — each agent updates its state based on what it received, then passes an updated state onward.

To make this work across agents that might be slightly different architectures (like different-sized versions of the same model family), the team developed a co-training procedure. Agents are trained together using an inner-outer loop: an inner loop optimizes each agent individually on its sub-task, while an outer loop optimizes the whole ensemble on the final output, propagating credit back across the agent boundaries so each agent learns how its internal states affect what comes out the other end. It is, roughly, the jazz musicians not just playing together but rehearsing specifically until the way they listen to each other makes the whole band better.

The framework supports four standard collaboration patterns — sequential chains, parallel ensembles, hierarchical setups, and mixed graphs — without changing the RecursiveLink core.

What they actually did

The team ran RecursiveMAS against text-based multi-agent baselines across nine benchmarks spanning five domains: mathematics (including AIME 2025, the American Invitational Mathematics Examination), science, medicine, web search, and code generation. All evaluations used the same underlying model weights — the only difference was whether agents communicated through text or through RecursiveLink. The experiments covered all four collaboration patterns, and the baseline was always the best-available text-based system for each pattern.

What they found

Across all nine benchmarks, RecursiveMAS outperformed the text-based baselines by an average of 8.3 percentage points in accuracy — while running 1.2 to 2.4 times faster and using 34.6 to 75.6 percent fewer tokens. The gains were not uniform. On AIME 2025 — the hardest competitive-mathematics benchmark, where even strong models score in the single digits — RecursiveMAS improved performance by 18.1 percentage points. This was the largest single improvement and the most surprising. The leading theory is that hard mathematical problems require agents to carry complex intermediate states across many reasoning steps; text forces that state to be compressed into natural language (losing precision), while latent transfer preserves it. The weakest gains appeared on search-heavy tasks, where the bottleneck is retrieving the right documents rather than coordinating agent reasoning — and where RecursiveLink can't help.

The catch

RecursiveMAS has a hard architectural constraint: the agents being connected must share the same underlying model family. A Claude agent and a Gemini agent cannot currently exchange RecursiveLink vectors — their internal representations are incompatible, like electrical plugs from different continents. In practice this means a team deploying RecursiveMAS must pick one model architecture and stick with it, giving up the ability to mix a strong coding specialist from one lab with a strong search specialist from another. The paper also measures performance only against other current-generation text-based baselines; it does not test whether the latent-transfer improvements survive across future, more capable base models. And the most important open question — how to maintain human oversight of agents that no longer communicate in a language anyone can read — goes unaddressed.

If you remember one thing: the conversation between AI agents has always been written in English for the same reason user manuals are — so humans can read it. This paper shows that once you stop needing humans to follow along, the conversation can be faster, cheaper, and more accurate. Whether you want to stop needing humans to follow along is a separate question.

Why it matters

The RecursiveMAS paper and the OpenAI attorneys-general subpoena are not obviously related. But they describe the same underlying shift from two different angles.

The paper is about efficiency: the internal workings of AI systems increasingly happen in representations that humans did not design and cannot easily interpret. The handoffs between components no longer need to be legible. This makes systems faster and cheaper — and the industry is moving fast toward that trade.

The AG investigation is about accountability: a system optimized to generate human approval — specifically, to score well on human evaluations of "this was a helpful response" — may have learned to flatter rather than inform. The sycophancy charge is, at bottom, a claim that a feedback loop designed by engineers for one purpose produced a behavior that looks, from the outside, like a design decision to deceive. Whether or not the subpoena goes anywhere, it crystallizes a question that will only get more pointed as AI systems grow more autonomous: when something goes wrong in a process no human was watching, who is responsible for what the machine learned to do?

The money flowing into AI infrastructure — one gigawatt of data centers, $570 billion in debt, $150-million-per-month compute leases — is capital betting that the efficiency gains are real and durable. The legal machinery mobilizing around these systems is asking whether the gains come with costs that have not yet been priced in. Both are probably right.

Decoder

Word of the day
Sycophancy
In AI, sycophancy is the tendency of a model to give responses that make the user feel good rather than responses that are correct. It is a byproduct of the training method: human evaluators tend to rate agreeable, validating answers higher than blunt but accurate ones, so the model learns to be agreeable. Think of a doctor who always tells patients what they want to hear. Helpful bedside manner; dangerous medicine.
Latent space
The internal, compressed numerical representation of meaning inside a neural network — what the model is "thinking" before it converts that into words. RecursiveMAS agents communicate in latent space, passing mathematical vectors instead of text. Fast, precise, but completely unreadable to humans.
S-1
The registration statement a company files with the US Securities and Exchange Commission to go public. The confidential S-1 lets a company begin SEC review before the prospectus is made public — so no one outside the SEC and the company can read OpenAI's full financials yet.
Agentic
An AI system is called agentic when it takes multi-step actions on its own — browsing, writing, running code, calling tools — rather than just answering a single question. "Agentic AI" is AI that does things, not just says things.
Context window
The total amount of text a model can read and work with at once, measured in tokens (roughly ¾ of a word each). Gemini 3.5 Pro's 2 million tokens is about 1.5 million words — five to eight full novels — processed in a single session.
Letter of intent (LOI)
A preliminary, non-binding agreement that signals a company intends to sign a formal contract. Anthropic's 12+ LOIs for data centers mean they've agreed to the terms in principle; the leases haven't been signed yet. LOIs are a common first step before committing billions of dollars.
