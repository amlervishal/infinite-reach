The path of least resistance

The year's top machine-learning prize went to a team that proved giving a language model more freedom makes it think worse. On the same day, the most capable publicly-available AI model left everyone's subscription plan and started billing by the word.

The one thing

Starting at midnight Pacific on July 8, Claude Fable 5 — Anthropic's flagship model — no longer comes bundled into any subscription tier. Every Pro, Max, Team, and Enterprise subscriber who wants Fable 5 must pre-purchase usage credits and will be billed at $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output tokens. That is double the rate of Claude Opus 4.8, and the highest per-token price Anthropic has ever publicly charged. The company frames this as a billing restructure rather than a price increase — subscribers still have access, just not from their flat monthly fee — but for anyone who relied on it heavily, costs have become metered and unpredictable overnight.

Paper, explained: when freedom becomes a trap

"The Flexibility Trap: Rethinking the Value of Arbitrary Order in Diffusion Language Models" · Zanlin Ni, Shenzhi Wang, Gao Huang et al., Tsinghua University & Alibaba · ICML 2026 Outstanding Paper Award · arXiv 2601.15165

This paper just won the top award at the world's largest machine-learning conference by turning one of the most celebrated features of a new class of language models into a diagnosed flaw. The insight is clear enough to explain in a sentence, and surprising enough that thousands of researchers voted it the year's best work.

The problem, and why it's hard

Every language model you have used — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — writes text one word at a time, strictly left to right. Word one, then word two, then word three. It works, but it is slow in a particular way: each word has to wait for the one before it. You cannot start computing word five until word four is done.

A newer family of models, called diffusion language models (dLLMs), tried to fix this by borrowing a trick from AI image generators. Instead of writing left to right, they start with a completely scrambled sentence — every position filled with random noise — and gradually refine the whole thing at once, like a photograph developing in a darkroom. The payoff: you can generate an entire sentence in far fewer computational steps. And more importantly, you can fill in the words in any order you like — confident ones first, uncertain ones later. The model becomes the editor of its own draft.

Several dLLMs shipped in 2025–26 (LLaDA, MDLM, and others) marketed this "arbitrary order generation" as a key advantage: let the model choose which blanks to fill first, and it should fill them wisely, saving the hardest choices for when it has the most context. Nobody had rigorously tested whether that story held up on the tasks that matter most — maths, code, multi-step reasoning.

How it works — and where it goes wrong

Think of solving a maths problem as filling in a five-square crossword. Four of the squares have obvious answers once you know the clue; one square in the middle is a genuine fork — fill it one way and the answer is 42, fill it another way and the answer is 7. Call this the forking square. In a left-to-right model, you arrive at that square in sequence, you have to commit, and across multiple attempts the model is forced to explore both branches. In a diffusion model with arbitrary fill order, you can defer that square. You do all the confident clues first — which feels efficient — and when you finally arrive at the fork, every surrounding letter is already locked in. The apparent freedom turns into a corridor. You guess, but the guess is constrained by what you chose earlier, so you never genuinely explore the alternatives.

The Tsinghua team called these critical junctions high-uncertainty forking tokens. Their central finding: dLLMs given free choice of fill order systematically defer exactly these tokens. The model is not lazy — it is doing precisely what its training told it to do, filling confident positions first. But the effect on hard reasoning tasks is that solution diversity collapses. The model produces an answer without ever genuinely weighing the alternatives. This is the flexibility trap: the freedom to choose order leads the model to choose the path of least resistance, and that path bypasses the most load-bearing decisions in the chain.

Their solution, JustGRPO, is almost provocatively simple. During training, remove the freedom: force the model to generate left to right, using a reinforcement-learning technique called GRPO that rewards correct answers. This makes the model confront every forking token in sequence and learn to handle it directly. At inference time — when you actually use the model — restore the diffusion process fully. Every token runs in parallel, just as before. The training phase teaches the model to reason through hard junctions; the inference phase keeps it fast.

What they actually did

The team took a standard dLLM backbone and applied JustGRPO with left-to-right trajectory constraints during the RL training phase. They then compared it against the best existing diffusion-specific training methods on a standard suite of maths and reasoning benchmarks. The key question: does it matter whether the RL exploration happens left-to-right or in arbitrary order?

What they found

On GSM8K — a set of maths word problems used as a benchmark for basic reasoning — JustGRPO reached 89.1% accuracy, compared to 86.1% for the best existing diffusion-specific training method. On MATH-500, a harder set of competition-style problems, it hit 45.1%. Both numbers came with parallel diffusion inference fully intact — no slowdown at test time. The paper's ablations confirm that the gain comes specifically from the left-to-right training constraint, not from other components of JustGRPO. The failure mode of arbitrary-order RL is also quantified: models trained that way produce significantly less diverse solution sets across multiple attempts — the mathematical signature of a model that never truly encountered a fork.

The catch

The result applies specifically to generation tasks that require chained reasoning — maths, code, logic problems. For tasks where fill-in-the-blank flexibility genuinely helps, like completing a sentence with a missing middle phrase or editing a document, arbitrary order may still be the right choice; the paper makes no claim there. The gains on maths are real but moderate — 3 percentage points on GSM8K does not close the full gap between the best dLLMs and the best autoregressive models. And the paper does not tell us whether dLLMs trained with JustGRPO generalise better to truly novel problems, or whether the result will hold as model scale increases. It is a precise diagnosis and a precise fix, not a general theory of reasoning.

If you remember one thing: Giving a language model freedom to write its words in any order sounds like an upgrade — but on hard reasoning tasks, it lets the model quietly dodge the most important decisions; training it to face them in order, even briefly, is enough to fix the problem.

Why it matters

Two stories this week seem unrelated but describe the same trap. Diffusion language models were given a feature — arbitrary word-order generation — that looked like pure upside: speed, flexibility, no forced commitment. It turned out to carry a hidden cost: the model used its freedom to avoid the hardest work. The fix was to remove the freedom during training, and restore it only when speed was needed. Fable 5's billing change follows the same logic: what looked like unrestricted access bundled into a flat fee is now explicitly metered. In both cases, apparent flexibility masked a tradeoff that only became legible once people pushed against it. That is a recurring pattern in how new technology actually lands: the thing that looked like freedom turns out to have a structure, and once the structure becomes visible, it can be worked with honestly.

Decoder

Word of the day
Diffusion model
An AI that starts from pure random noise and gradually clarifies it into something meaningful — used for images since 2022, and now increasingly for text. Think of it as a photograph developing in a darkroom: the image doesn't appear one pixel at a time from the top corner; the whole thing slowly sharpens from a grey blur, all at once.
Forking token
A word or symbol in a sequence where the text could go in genuinely different directions. Committing one way here determines much of what follows — it is the crux of a reasoning step, the crossword square where the hardest choices live.
Autoregressive
The standard way a language model generates text: one token at a time, left to right, where each new word can only look at everything before it. Reliable, but impossible to parallelise.
Token
The basic unit of text an AI processes — roughly one word, or a fragment of a longer word. "Unbelievable" might be split into three tokens. Models generate or score text one token at a time.
GRPO
Group Relative Policy Optimization — a reinforcement-learning technique that trains a model to prefer outputs that score better on a defined reward (such as a correct maths answer), without needing a separate scoring model. JustGRPO is a variant applied to diffusion LLMs.
Usage credits
Prepaid tokens bought separately from a monthly subscription. Instead of a flat fee granting unlimited (or rate-limited) access, you pay per million tokens consumed. Standard for API access; as of today, also required for Fable 5 consumer use.
