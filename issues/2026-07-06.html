What fewer choices unlock

The world's fastest frontier model is arriving this month at a speed that would have sounded fictional six months ago. And this week's biggest machine-learning conference gave its top prize to a study that found the opposite of what the field expected: giving an AI model more freedom to think makes it think worse.

The one thing

OpenAI confirmed that GPT-5.6 Sol — its most capable model, currently in limited preview — will run on Cerebras hardware at up to 750 tokens per second in July. A standard GPU cluster serves a frontier model at 40–120 tokens per second; Cerebras uses a single chip the size of a dinner plate to go roughly an order of magnitude faster. At 750 tokens per second, a thousand-word answer arrives in about 1.3 seconds. That closes the gap between human turn-taking rhythms and model response time — which changes what you can actually use these systems for in real time. Access stays limited to a small set of government-vetted partners for now, with broader rollout coming as Cerebras scales capacity.

Paper, explained: why giving AI more freedom makes it dumber

"The Flexibility Trap: Rethinking the Value of Arbitrary Order in Diffusion Language Models" · Zanlin Ni et al., Tsinghua University & Alibaba Group · arXiv:2601.15165 · ICML 2026 Distinguished Paper Award (Oral)

This paper won the top research prize at ICML 2026, and its central finding is exactly backwards from what the field assumed: the feature that makes diffusion language models special — the freedom to generate words in any order — is the very thing that stops them from reasoning well.

The problem, and why it's hard

Almost every language model you've used writes text the same way a human types: left to right, one word at a time. Each new word is predicted based on everything written before it. This works well, but it has a structural limitation: the model commits to each word as it goes and can't revise earlier choices.

Diffusion language models — dLLMs, in the shorthand — were built to change that. Borrowed from the technology behind image generators like DALL-E and Midjourney, they start from a page of random noise and gradually refine it into a coherent answer. Crucially, they can generate tokens in any order: write the easy parts first, skip the hard parts for later, hop around wherever the model wants. This property, called arbitrary order generation, was considered a major advantage. More flexibility should mean better, more creative reasoning — the model can think freely rather than being locked into a single left-to-right path.

The paper argues the opposite is true.

How it works

Think of a student taking a multiple-choice test who is allowed to answer questions in any order. Most students use this sensibly: tackle the easy ones first, come back to the hard ones later. But here is the subtler problem the researchers identified. For reasoning chains — not isolated facts, but connected arguments — the hard parts are not optional. They are where the logic turns.

In a chain of reasoning, words like "therefore," "since," and "if and only if" are pivot points: the moments where one idea leads to another, where a conclusion is justified, where a branch in the argument is taken. Logicians call these high-uncertainty tokens — they are uncertain because the model doesn't yet know which way the reasoning will go. They are also, for exactly this reason, the most important words in the chain.

When a diffusion language model is given arbitrary order freedom, it does what any rational agent does when given a choice: it avoids the hardest parts and fills in the comfortable ones first. By the time it returns to write "therefore" or "since," all the surrounding words are already fixed — the context has already narrowed the space. The model fills in the pivot word to fit what's already there, rather than using it to genuinely navigate a branch in the reasoning. It commits to one answer before it has properly explored others.

The researchers call this premature collapse of solution coverage: the model is technically flexible, but in practice it uses that flexibility to avoid the intellectual work that flexibility was supposed to help with.

The fix, called JustGRPO, is almost embarrassingly simple. During training, the model is forced to generate tokens in the normal left-to-right order — the same sequential discipline that standard language models use. This forces it to encounter and learn from every pivot token, in context, rather than deferring them. At inference (actual deployment), the model still generates tokens in parallel — so it keeps all the speed advantages of the diffusion architecture. The constraint applies only during training. The implementation is roughly 60 lines of code, using an existing reinforcement learning algorithm called GRPO with no modifications.

What they actually did

The team applied JustGRPO to existing diffusion language models and evaluated them on four standard benchmarks: GSM8K (grade-school math word problems), MATH-500 (competition-level mathematics), HumanEval, and MBPP (both coding tasks). They compared JustGRPO against prior methods specifically engineered for arbitrary-order diffusion training — approaches with substantially more complexity.

What they found

JustGRPO reached 89.1% on GSM8K at a generation length of 256 tokens, matching or beating more elaborate diffusion-specific methods. On MATH-500: 45.1%. On coding: 49.4% on HumanEval and 52.4% on MBPP. A LoRA-trained variant (a lightweight fine-tuning technique that modifies only a small fraction of the model's weights) scored 89.6% on GSM8K. The pattern held across all four benchmarks: the simplest method won, and the winner was the one that threw away the supposed advantage of diffusion models in the first place.

The catch

These results are on reasoning benchmarks — math and code — where there are clear right answers to check. Whether the same "freedom trap" applies to creative writing, open-ended summarization, or other unstructured tasks hasn't been tested. The paper also studies a specific class of diffusion language models; it doesn't claim arbitrary order is always worse everywhere, only that for reasoning, the assumed benefit appears absent. And 45.1% on MATH-500, while good for a diffusion model, is still well behind the best autoregressive models in the same period. This is a result that challenges a design assumption, not a result that declares diffusion language models competitive at the frontier.

If you remember one thing: When you give an AI model the freedom to answer in any order, it uses that freedom to avoid the hardest parts of the question — and a 60-line fix that takes that freedom away makes it reason better.

Why it matters

Two threads this week point in the same direction, from opposite sides. The ICML Distinguished Paper shows that adding options to a system — giving it more degrees of freedom — can make it perform worse, because unconstrained systems often take the lazy path instead of the necessary one. The model's freedom to skip hard tokens turned out to be a freedom to reason shallowly. Meanwhile, the Cerebras speed story shows that a different kind of freedom — freedom from the constraint of slow response time — genuinely unlocks new things. A model that responds in 1.3 seconds can participate in real conversations in a way a 10-second model cannot.

The pattern worth watching: as AI systems become more capable, the question of where to apply constraints and where to remove them becomes more important than raw scale. The JustGRPO authors didn't build a bigger model. They noticed a design assumption that nobody had questioned, tested it, found it backwards, and fixed it with 60 lines. That kind of result — architecture insight beating parameter count — tends to matter more over time, not less.

Decoder

Word of the day
Diffusion model
A type of AI that starts from pure random noise and gradually "cleans it up" into a meaningful output — like developing a photograph in a darkroom, but working backwards from static. Image generators like DALL-E and Midjourney use this approach; diffusion language models apply the same idea to text.
Arbitrary order generation
A diffusion language model's ability to write tokens in any sequence rather than left to right. Seemed like an advantage; turns out to enable shallow reasoning.
Pivot token
A word in a reasoning chain — like "therefore" or "since" — that marks a logical turn. Miss these in training and the model learns to reason around its hardest moments rather than through them.
GRPO
Group Relative Policy Optimization: a reinforcement learning algorithm that teaches a model to prefer answers that scored better in a comparison group. JustGRPO uses it without modification — hence the name.
Wafer-scale chip
Cerebras's unusual hardware design: rather than slicing a silicon wafer into dozens of small processors, they use the entire wafer as one giant chip. The result is massive on-chip memory and unprecedented token throughput for large models.
Token throughput
The rate (tokens per second) at which a model produces output. Standard GPU clusters: 40–120 t/s for a frontier model. Cerebras with GPT-5.6 Sol: up to 750 t/s — a qualitative shift in response experience.
