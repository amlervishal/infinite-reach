What the hand no longer writes

A quiet set of numbers emerged this week: more than 80% of new code shipped into production at one of the world's most scrutinised labs was written by its own model — up from low single digits in early 2025. Meanwhile, a Penn Engineering team published a paper that solves one of science's hardest computation problems by borrowing a mathematical trick from the 1940s.

The one thing

Anthropic published figures this week showing that as of May 2026, more than 80% of the code merged into its primary production codebase was authored by Claude — its own model. In February 2025, before Claude Code launched, that number was in the low single digits. The company reports its engineers are now merging roughly eight times as much code per day as in 2024. As a concrete example: Claude autonomously shipped over 800 individual fixes for a persistent API error class, reducing the error rate by a factor of 1,000 — work Anthropic estimates would have taken four human-years to complete. Co-founder Jack Clark said in an interview that reaching 100% autonomous code is possible within two years.

Paper, explained: teaching physics models to stop amplifying noise

Mollifier Layers: Enabling Efficient High-Order Derivatives in Inverse PDE Learning · Vinayak Vinayak, Ananyae Kumar Bhartari, Vivek B. Shenoy · Penn Engineering · arXiv 2505.11682

Penn Engineering published a paper in Transactions on Machine Learning Research that makes AI-driven physics models six to ten times more efficient — using a mathematical idea that has been sitting in textbooks since the 1940s. It is the kind of result that reads like a correction rather than an invention: something that, in retrospect, should have been tried earlier.

The problem, and why it's hard

Many scientific questions require working backwards from data to hidden causes. You have a grid of temperature readings across a room and want to know the thermal conductivity equation driving them. You have chemical concentrations measured across a tissue sample and want to recover the reaction rates that produced them. This "working backwards" problem is called an inverse PDE problem. (A PDE — partial differential equation — is the language science uses to describe anything that varies through both space and time: heat, flow, diffusion, elasticity.)

The standard AI approach for inverse PDEs is called a Physics-Informed Neural Network, or PINN. The network learns a function that fits your data, and you bake the governing equation directly into the training loss — essentially penalising the network whenever its output violates the physics. To check whether it's violating the physics, you need the network's derivatives with respect to its inputs: rate of change, curvature, and so on. The tool for computing those derivatives is automatic differentiation (autodiff), which traces the chain rule back through the network layer by layer.

For a first-order equation, this is fine. But many important equations — diffusion, reaction-diffusion, structural mechanics — are second, third, or fourth order, meaning you have to differentiate through an already-differentiated network, repeatedly. Each pass amplifies numerical noise and roughly doubles the memory required. The Penn team's benchmark made the severity concrete: adding a fourth-order PDE loss to a reaction-diffusion problem pushed peak memory from 0.21 gigabytes to 2.70 gigabytes — a thirteen-fold increase. Training slows. Convergence degrades. At high enough order, the model simply cannot learn.

How it works

Here is the core analogy: imagine you are trying to measure how steeply a hillside slopes, using a map whose elevation readings have random speckle noise on every pixel. If you compute the slope directly — subtracting adjacent readings — the noise dominates. But if you first apply a gentle blur to the map and then measure the slope, the noise averages away and the gradient is clean and stable. The blur you apply is called a mollifier in mathematics — a smooth, bell-shaped kernel that was formalised by the French mathematician Laurent Schwartz in the late 1940s as part of distribution theory.

Mollifier Layers do exactly this, but for neural network outputs. Before computing any derivatives, the method slides a smooth Gaussian-like kernel over the network's raw output using convolution. Crucially: because the mollifier kernel is analytically defined (a known, smooth mathematical function rather than something learned), its own derivatives are available in closed form. That means you can compute the derivatives of the smoothed output directly using the convolution theorem — no recursive autodiff required at all for those high-order terms. The recursive, noise-amplifying cascade is replaced by a single, stable convolution step.

The module slots in as a thin layer between the network's output and the PDE loss. It requires no change to the underlying network architecture — PINNs, DeepONets, neural operators, anything works. The only hyperparameter introduced is the mollifier's bandwidth: how wide the blur kernel is. Too narrow and noise creeps back; too wide and real structure gets erased. In practice, the bandwidth is chosen once per problem class and held fixed.

What they actually did

Vinayak Vinayak, Ananyae Kumar Bhartari, and Vivek B. Shenoy (senior author, Eduardo D. Glandt President's Distinguished Professor at Penn) tested Mollifier Layers on a benchmark suite spanning first-, second-, and fourth-order PDEs: Langevin dynamics (stochastic particle motion), heat diffusion, and reaction-diffusion systems. They compared against standard autodiff-based PINNs on both memory usage and accuracy of recovered parameters. They also applied the method to a real-world inverse problem: using super-resolution microscopy images of cell nuclei to infer spatially varying epigenetic reaction rates — the rates at which genes switch on and off in different regions of a chromosome.

What they found

Across the benchmark suite, Mollifier Layers cut memory footprint and training time by 6 to 10 times. Accuracy of parameter recovery improved substantially for fourth-order problems, where standard autodiff methods failed to converge reliably. On the chromatin imaging application, the method successfully recovered spatially varying rate constants that conventional PINNs could not estimate stably. The paper is published in Transactions on Machine Learning Research and is scheduled for presentation at NeurIPS 2026.

The catch

The method has been validated on a carefully chosen set of PDE types in controlled settings. Whether it holds up across the enormous variety of real-world inverse problems — seismology, aerodynamics, plasma physics — is untested. The bandwidth hyperparameter, while simple in principle, requires some tuning for each new problem class; the paper does not provide an automatic selection procedure. The authors also note that very sparse observation data (only a handful of measurement points) may not give the convolution enough local context to smooth effectively. This is a research paper, not a production tool.

If you remember one thing: You can make AI physics models 6–10 times faster and more accurate — not by redesigning the network, but by blurring its output before measuring, using a 1940s mathematical trick.

Why it matters

The Anthropic statistic — 80% autonomous code — is easy to misread. It's not a claim that the software is finished or that human engineers are redundant. What it actually shows is that the definition of "writing code" has already shifted. The eight-times productivity multiplier means engineers are doing more, not fewer things. The 800-fix API repair job is the clearest illustration: no human team was going to spend four years on a single error class. The AI didn't replace the decision to fix it; it just made the fixing tractable.

The OpenAI-Ona acquisition fits the same pattern from a different angle. Coding agents that run only while your laptop is open are useful. Agents that keep working overnight, in a cloud sandbox, with persistent state and memory, are a different category of tool. The acquisition suggests OpenAI sees the shift from "assistant you call" to "colleague who keeps going" as the next commercial frontier in developer tooling.

And the Mollifier Layers paper, modest as it sounds, belongs in this context too. The bottleneck it removes is exactly the kind that slows down physics AI at the frontier: not compute, not data, but numerical stability. Making the machinery more reliable is how you go from proof-of-concept to deployment in materials science, drug discovery, and climate modeling. Progress in those fields increasingly depends on incremental unblockings like this one.

Decoder

Word of the day
Mollifier
A smooth, bell-shaped mathematical function used to "tame" rough signals. In the 1940s, Laurent Schwartz formalised the idea as a way to take derivatives of functions that aren't technically differentiable — you blur the function slightly first, take the derivative of the smooth version, then let the blur shrink to zero. Think of it as pre-cleaning a noisy sensor reading before you try to measure how fast it's changing.
Inverse PDE
Working backwards: you observe the result (temperature readings, chemical concentrations) and want to recover the governing equation's hidden parameters. The "forward" problem — given the equation, predict the result — is easier; the inverse is hard because many different equations can produce similar observations.
Autodiff
Automatic differentiation — software that traces through a computation and applies the chain rule automatically to produce exact gradients. Used in every modern neural network training; becomes numerically unstable and memory-hungry when applied recursively for high-order derivatives.
PINN
Physics-Informed Neural Network — a model trained to fit observed data and satisfy a known governing equation simultaneously. The equation acts as a constraint in the loss function, keeping the network's predictions physically plausible even between measurement points.
Recursive self-improvement
When an AI system improves the very tools and code that build future versions of itself. Not necessarily dramatic: Anthropic's 80% figure is a real-world instance — Claude writing Claude's own codebase is a mild, supervised form of this loop.
Context window
The maximum amount of text a language model can "see" at once — its working memory. Kimi K2.7-Code's 256,000-token window means it can hold roughly 200,000 words of code and conversation in mind simultaneously before it starts forgetting the beginning.
