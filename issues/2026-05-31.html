The mapmakers who keep drawing the same map

A study out of Hong Kong finds that AI research assistants, despite generating ideas by the thousand, tend to cluster around familiar territory rather than push into the unknown — a quiet counterweight to a week of billion-dollar bets and unprecedented machine capabilities.

The one thing

Britain's AI Safety Institute ran Anthropic's newest model — Claude Mythos Preview — through a gauntlet called "The Last Ones": a 32-step simulated enterprise cyberattack that no previous AI had completed from start to finish. Mythos cleared it. On expert-level security tasks that no model could handle before April 2025, it now succeeds 73% of the time. The Institute called this the "autonomous offensive threshold" — the point where an AI can, in principle, carry out a complex attack without a human directing each step. Anthropic confirmed a June public release and says new safeguards are being built before launch. It is the first time any government safety body has publicly said a model crosses that line.

Paper, explained: when AI scientists think too much alike

"AI Research Agents Narrow Scientific Exploration" · Yixuan Tang & Yi Yang · HKUST · arxiv.org/abs/2605.27905

Labs and universities have been building AI "scientists" — systems that read papers, generate hypotheses, and even design experiments. The assumption was that they'd help us explore more of the map. This paper, published last week, asks whether that's actually true. The answer, carefully measured across 37,802 generated ideas, is no.

The problem, and why it's hard

Science doesn't just need speed — it needs breadth. The discoveries that reshape fields often come from someone working at an unexpected angle: a physicist borrowing a technique from biology, a chemist chasing an odd outlier that everyone else ignored. The worry with automating research isn't that AI won't produce ideas, but that it'll produce ideas that are too similar to each other and too close to what already exists — efficient, but narrow.

Measuring breadth is surprisingly difficult. You can't ask a model to rate its own originality, and you can't simply count how many ideas it produced. Tang and Yang needed a way to compare the intellectual spread of thousands of AI-generated ideas against thousands of human-authored papers — to ask not "are these ideas good?" but "how much territory do they cover?"

How it works

Imagine scientific ideas as dots scattered on a map. Human researchers, over decades, spread those dots across a huge landscape — some clustered in busy areas, but many in lonely corners far from the crowd. The most important discoveries have almost always come from the lonely corners.

The authors built that map in mathematical form. They took a large collection of AI and machine-learning papers and converted each one into a vector — a string of numbers that captures its meaning: the subject it covers, the methods it uses, the vocabulary it reaches for. This places each paper as a dot in what mathematicians call a semantic space. Two papers about similar things end up close together; two papers from different corners of the field sit far apart. The distance between any two dots is measured with a tool called cosine similarity, a number between 0 and 1 where 1 means "virtually identical" and 0 means "nothing in common."

Then they ran four popular AI research-agent frameworks — the kind that search literature, synthesise findings, and draft new research directions — each backed by up to six different large language models including GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet. They fed every agent the same starting papers ("seed literature") from a defined research area and asked each to generate new research ideas. Result: 37,802 ideas in total.

The key measurement was average pairwise cosine similarity within each research area. Think of it as: if you connected every pair of AI-generated ideas with a line on the map, how short are those lines on average? Short lines mean ideas are bunched together — narrow exploration. Long lines mean ideas are spread out — wide exploration. They compared those numbers against the same metric computed for actual human-authored papers from the same research areas.

What they actually did

The study spanned multiple citation-defined research areas inside AI and machine learning. For each area, they ran all four agent frameworks across the six language models — 37,802 total generated ideas — and compared the spread against three baselines: (1) human-authored papers from the same area, (2) follow-on human work that emerged from the same seed literature, and (3) the seed literature itself. Getting all three baselines right mattered: comparing only against the original papers would have unfairly penalised agents for staying on topic, so the authors also tracked what human researchers produced when they, too, started from the same seeds.

What they found

Four consistent patterns emerged across every combination of agent and model tested. First, AI-generated ideas were systematically more similar to each other than human papers from the same area — their cosine similarity was higher, meaning their dots on the map were bunched closer together. Second, this narrowing held regardless of which agent framework or underlying language model was used — it wasn't a quirk of one system. Third, the regions where AI ideas clustered tended to correspond to parts of the existing literature with lower citation counts — not the influential, high-impact corners, but the safer, more-covered ground. Fourth, AI ideas were more similar to the seed literature than human follow-on work was. In other words: agents hugged the prompt. Humans pushed past it.

There were no exceptions. Every single combination of agent plus model narrowed relative to human researchers.

The catch

The study is limited to AI and machine learning — a field where agent frameworks have been most extensively developed and where training data is dense. Whether the same narrowing appears in materials science, ecology, or medicine is unknown. The paper also measures the distribution of ideas, not their eventual quality: it can't say whether any given AI idea was better or worse than a human one, only that the full set was less varied. And cosine similarity in a learned semantic space is a proxy for intellectual distance — useful and principled, but imperfect. An idea can look semantically close to existing work and still open an entirely new direction in practice.

If you remember one thing: AI research agents produce many ideas quickly, but those ideas cluster tighter than human thinking does — which is precisely the opposite of how science makes its largest leaps.

Why it matters

Two stories this week pull in opposite directions, and both matter. The first is consolidation: a small number of labs are absorbing almost all the capital — Anthropic near a trillion, OpenAI heading toward a public listing, both deploying specialised frontier models (Rosalind, Mythos) to governments that are now building real infrastructure on top of them. These tools are no longer experimental; they are load-bearing.

The second story is quieter. The paper from Hong Kong doesn't say AI agents are useless. It says they are efficient in a particular way — fast, productive, and concentrated. They generate ideas near the centre of mass, not at the edges. For the incremental work that fills most of a research programme, that's probably fine, and may even be valuable. But science's most important moments have almost always come from the periphery — from someone who wasn't supposed to be working on that problem at all, following a hunch that didn't fit the prevailing frame. If AI agents become the primary engine of idea generation, and those agents systematically avoid the periphery, something important may quietly erode before anyone notices it is gone.

Decoder

Word of the day
Semantic space
A mathematical map of meaning. Every piece of text — a word, a sentence, a whole paper — gets converted into a list of numbers (called an embedding) that places it as a dot on this map. Dots close together have similar meanings; dots far apart are unrelated. The map is learned from vast amounts of text, so it encodes the associations humans have built up through all that writing. Researchers use it to measure how similar two ideas are without reading both of them by hand.
Cosine similarity
A way to measure how close two points in semantic space are: 1.0 means identical, 0 means completely unrelated. It measures the angle between two vectors rather than their length, so long papers and short papers can still be compared fairly.
Embedding
The numeric coordinates of a piece of text in semantic space — typically a list of hundreds or thousands of numbers. Converting words into these coordinates is what lets computers compare meaning by measuring distances.
Research agent
An AI system designed to conduct parts of the scientific process autonomously: searching literature, synthesising findings, proposing hypotheses, writing or running code. The "AI scientist" concept, in short.
Autonomous offensive threshold
The point at which an AI can carry out a complex, multi-step cyberattack without a human directing each individual step. The UK AI Safety Institute used this term to describe what Claude Mythos Preview crossed in their evaluation.
Series H
The eighth major round of private investment a company raises (rounds are named alphabetically). By Series H, a company is typically enormous; reaching it at Anthropic's scale is extremely rare and usually signals an IPO is close.
