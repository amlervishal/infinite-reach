Eighty years, one question, one night

An AI model cracks a problem in pure mathematics that had sat unanswered since 1946, OpenAI publishes a formal rulebook for its most dangerous systems, and Europe fields its first serious transatlantic AI challenger. Here is Saturday's issue.

The one thing

On May 20, OpenAI revealed that an internal, experimental AI reasoning model had disproved a conjecture in pure mathematics posed by the Hungarian mathematician Paul Erdős in 1946 — a question nobody had resolved in 80 years. The problem asks: given n points scattered on a flat plane, what is the maximum number of pairs of points that are exactly the same distance apart? Erdős believed the best you could do was a neat square grid. The AI found a completely different kind of arrangement — drawing on tools from algebraic number theory, a branch of mathematics nobody had thought to connect to this problem — and produced a construction that beats the grid for large enough n. Nine mathematicians, including Fields Medalist Tim Gowers, independently verified the 125-page proof. More below.

Paper, explained: the geometry problem that took eighty years

Unit Distance Problem / Erdős Conjecture · OpenAI internal research · openai.com

You'll never read the 125-page proof. So we read the coverage — and the mathematics — for you.

Why it caught our eye

An AI model was handed a single open question — one that had sat unanswered for 80 years — and came back with an answer. Not a better approximation, not a partial result: a complete disproof, verified by nine world-class mathematicians including a Fields Medalist. The method it used surprised even the people who checked it.

The problem, and why it's hard

Here's the question Erdős asked in 1946: if you place n points anywhere on a flat surface, what is the maximum number of pairs of points that are exactly the same distance apart? Call that distance "1 unit" — a metre, a centimetre, anything you like. How many of your points can simultaneously be exactly 1 apart from some other point in the group?

For small n, you can experiment by hand. For a million points, it becomes fiendishly hard. Adding a new point creates new pairs but can also force earlier ones apart; the geometry of the whole arrangement shifts. The best construction anyone found, going back to Erdős himself, was a regular square grid. Erdős conjectured this was essentially optimal: the true maximum grew no faster than a certain formula tied to the grid. For 80 years, nobody found a better construction — and nobody proved you couldn't, either.

How it works

The AI's key move was to reach into a completely different branch of mathematics: algebraic number theory, the study of numbers that are roots of polynomial equations (like √2 or the cube root of 5). Its connection to flat-plane geometry is not obvious. Nobody in discrete geometry had thought to use it here.

Think of it this way. Imagine you're arranging hooks on a wall so that every pair of hooks is exactly one metre apart. You can do this with a regular grid — plain rows and columns. That's the Erdős approach. Now imagine instead you use a rule from chemistry: position every hook according to where atoms sit in a particular crystal lattice. The atomic structure dictates the spacings, and if you choose the right crystal, the rules pack in far more "exactly-one-metre" pairs than any grid ever could — once the arrangement is large enough.

The AI used a structure called an infinite class field tower, from a technique in number theory called Golod-Shafarevich theory. A class field tower is a way of building up a number system in layers, each inheriting constraints from the one below. When you map the coordinates of this tower onto a flat plane, the underlying algebraic relationships force an unusually high density of unit-distance pairs into existence. The crucial property the AI proved: the growth rate carries a fixed positive exponent — specifically, δ = 0.014, as Princeton mathematician Will Sawin later refined it. A fixed positive exponent beats a shrinking one. For large enough n, the AI's construction produces strictly more unit-distance pairs than any square grid.

What they actually did

An unnamed experimental internal OpenAI reasoning model was given the unit distance problem as a single open-ended prompt. No hints. No intermediate scaffolding. The model worked through the proof itself, producing 125 pages of step-by-step reasoning — constructing an infinite family of point arrangements using class field towers, then proving they achieve n1+δ unit-distance pairs. OpenAI released the full proof text, the model's reasoning chain, and a non-specialist summary. Will Sawin posted a companion refinement, pinning the exponent at δ = 0.014.

What they found

The Erdős conjecture was wrong. There exist arrangements of points — built from algebraic number theory — that achieve strictly more unit-distance pairs than a square grid, by a fixed polynomial factor. Nine independent mathematicians verified the proof. Fields Medalist Tim Gowers is among them. Greg Brockman of OpenAI described it as "the first time AI has autonomously solved a prominent open problem central to a field of mathematics." What surprised mathematicians most was not the result itself — it's that the solution came from a completely unexpected direction.

The catch

The improvement is real but theoretically modest: δ = 0.014 means the AI's construction only overtakes the square grid for values of n that are, in practice, astronomically large — far beyond anything you could draw, compute with, or apply to a real problem. This is pure mathematics; it changes no software, no algorithm, no application. It may never. The proof is also publicly released and mathematician-verified, but has not yet appeared in a peer-reviewed journal.

If you remember one thing: an AI model, handed a single unanswered question from 1946, found the answer by borrowing tools from a completely unrelated branch of mathematics — and even the experts who verified it were surprised by the bridge it built.

Decoder

Word of the day
Conjecture
A claim in mathematics that everyone believes is probably true — but nobody has yet proved (or disproved). Like a bridge that looks solid but nobody's driven over it. Sometimes, after decades, someone finds the bridge isn't there at all.
Unit distance
Two points that are exactly the same distance apart — "1 unit," whatever you've defined as a unit. The unit distance problem counts how many such pairs you can pack among n points.
Discrete geometry
The branch of maths studying arrangements of separate, countable points or objects — not smooth curves but finite sets and the patterns they form.
Class field tower
A layered construction from number theory: think of building a number system floor by floor, where each floor inherits algebraic rules from the one below. The AI used this to build point arrangements with an unusual density of equal-distance pairs.
Polynomial improvement
When a quantity grows by a fixed power (like n^1.014) rather than by a factor that shrinks over time. In this context: the AI's construction makes more unit-distance pairs per point than any grid, for large enough n.
Reasoning model
An AI that works through a problem step by step before giving an answer — producing a long chain of intermediate reasoning rather than jumping straight to a conclusion.
Sovereign AI
AI infrastructure owned or controlled by a non-US entity — the term governments use for the goal of not depending entirely on American providers.
