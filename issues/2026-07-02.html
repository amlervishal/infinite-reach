Conditions of release

Three weeks ago, the US government forced the most capable model in the world offline over a security flaw. Yesterday it came back — and the terms of its return sketch what powerful-technology governance may look like for the next decade.

The one thing

Claude Fable 5 — Anthropic's flagship model and the most capable publicly available AI — was restored to global users on July 1 after the US Department of Commerce lifted an export-control order it had imposed on June 12. The original ban followed a jailbreak discovered by Amazon researchers: they found a way to bypass Fable 5's guardrails and get it to write working exploit code for software vulnerabilities. The suspension lasted nineteen days. The conditions for lifting it are notable: Anthropic agreed to proactively detect and address security risks in its models, to work with the government on protocols for future major releases, and to report any malicious activity it finds among users. Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick framed the resolution as Anthropic "no longer requiring an export license" — which means the model itself changed, or the company's commitments did, or both. Either way, a new playbook now exists: government can ban a frontier model, negotiate, and un-ban it under conditions. That cycle has now run once to completion.

Paper, explained: teaching a new kind of model to go seven times faster

SimSD: Simple Speculative Decoding in Diffusion Language Models · arXiv preprint · arxiv.org/abs/2606.02544

A June 2026 paper quietly solved a speed bottleneck that's been frustrating researchers working on a new family of language models — and the fix required no retraining, just a small change to how one matrix is set up. The result: 7.46 times more text generated per second, with quality that held or improved.

The problem, and why it's hard

There are two main ways language models generate text right now. The kind most people use — ChatGPT, Claude, Llama — is called autoregressive: the model writes one word at a time, left to right, each word depending on everything before it. It's reliable but slow. Every additional word requires a full pass through the model's billions of parameters.

A newer family called diffusion language models (dLLMs for short) works differently. Instead of writing left to right, they start with a sequence of blank placeholder tokens — imagine a page of underscore characters — and iteratively fill them in. Each pass resolves more of the blanks, converging on the final text after several rounds. Because many blanks can be resolved in parallel, dLLMs are naturally faster in theory. They've been getting serious research attention as an alternative to the standard approach.

Now here's the problem. Over the last few years, a powerful acceleration trick called speculative decoding has made autoregressive models significantly faster. The idea: use a tiny "draft" model to quickly sketch out a run of five or eight tokens, then pass that draft to the big model. Because the big model runs in parallel across all positions, it can check the entire draft in one sweep — and if it agrees with the first four tokens but not the fifth, it keeps the first four and discards the rest. In practice, this often yields three or four tokens for the price of a single verification step. Speed gains of two to four times are common.

The problem is that diffusion models cannot use this trick. Standard speculative decoding relies on causal masking — the attention structure that prevents each token from looking at what comes after it, so the model can give a clean verdict on whether a drafted token is correct given only its left-hand context. Diffusion models use bidirectional attention: every token can see every other token simultaneously. That's why they can fill in blanks in parallel. But it also means the context a diffusion model uses to evaluate any given token keeps changing as other blanks get filled. You can't cleanly isolate "what does the model think about this one token" because the answer depends on the state of all the others. Standard speculative decoding simply breaks.

How it works

SimSD's insight is that you don't need to change the diffusion model — you need to change what the drafted tokens are allowed to see.

Think of it this way. Imagine you're a senior editor checking whether a junior writer correctly filled in the blanks in a crossword puzzle. Normally in a crossword, each answer depends on the crossing letters — so to check 8-Across, you'd need to see how 3-Down and 11-Down overlap with it. That mutual dependency makes it hard to check any single answer independently. But if you could temporarily cover up all the crossing letters and just ask "does this word look right given only its clue?", you'd get a clean verdict — and you could do that for every blank simultaneously.

That's essentially what SimSD does. For each token the draft model has proposed, it constructs a special attention mask — a matrix that controls which positions the model is allowed to look at when evaluating that draft token. Specifically, it hides the other draft tokens from each other's view, restoring something like left-to-right causal context for just the drafted positions. The draft tokens become what the paper calls "reference tokens" — they sit in the input alongside the current denoising step, but a carefully designed mask keeps them temporally separated. This gives the main diffusion model the context it needs to produce valid "agree or disagree" signals for each drafted token in a single forward pass.

Crucially, none of this requires touching the model's weights. The mask is computed at the attention layer and discarded. SimSD is entirely plug-and-play: it works on top of any existing diffusion language model, stacks with other speed techniques like KV caching, and adds no training cost.

What they actually did

The team tested SimSD on the SDAR family of diffusion language models — models that were converted from standard autoregressive architectures via full-model training on approximately 50 billion tokens to enable parallel "block" generation. They evaluated across four standard benchmarks covering language understanding and generation quality. They combined SimSD with two other acceleration techniques: KV caching (which stores and reuses certain intermediate computations across steps) and blockwise decoding (which processes multiple positions per step). This made SimSD a component in a layered speedup stack rather than a standalone trick.

What they found

In the best configuration, the system achieved 7.46 times higher decoding throughput compared to the baseline diffusion model running without speculative decoding. On the four benchmarks, generation quality held steady and in some cases improved slightly — a useful surprise that the authors attribute to the draft model's proposals steering the main model toward coherent completions. Because the draft model is small and fast, its guesses don't slow things down even when the main model rejects them.

The catch

The 7.46x figure is measured under specific conditions: SDAR-family models, four particular benchmarks, with KV cache and blockwise decoding also active. Throughput in real deployments will depend on draft model quality, hardware, and batch size — typical real-world gains would likely be lower but still substantial. The paper doesn't test SimSD on the very largest frontier-scale models, and diffusion language models are still maturing as a category; almost no production applications run on them today. The paper also doesn't compare SimSD against the most competitive speculative decoding variants for autoregressive models, so it's hard to say how close the two families now are in raw speed.

If you remember one thing: SimSD found a way to donate autoregressive models' best speed trick to diffusion models — training-free — by redesigning just one matrix in the attention layer.

Why it matters

The Fable 5 episode is the first time the full enforcement cycle has run to completion for a commercial AI model: a government imposed export controls, the company negotiated, the controls were lifted under conditions, and the product returned. That playbook now exists. Future incidents — and there will be future incidents — will reference it. The specific conditions Anthropic agreed to (proactive detection, protocol coordination, malicious-activity reporting) are also a window into what the US government currently considers an adequate safety posture for a frontier model. They're not onerous — but they're also not nothing. Meanwhile, the SimSD paper is a reminder that the competitive dynamics in AI aren't only about which model is biggest. A new generation of models based on completely different mathematical principles is getting faster every month, and the gap between them and the standard approach is narrowing from a different direction than most people are watching.

Decoder

Word of the day
Speculative decoding
A speed trick for AI text generation: a small fast model guesses the next several words, then the big model checks all the guesses at once. If the guesses are mostly right, you get five words for the price of one check. Like having an assistant draft a paragraph and then skimming it to see if you'd have written the same thing — much faster than writing it yourself word by word.
Diffusion language model
A text-generation approach that starts with a sequence of blank placeholder tokens and iteratively fills them in over several passes, rather than writing left to right one word at a time. Faster in theory and increasingly competitive in benchmarks, but not yet the default in deployed products.
Autoregressive model
The standard type: generates one token at a time, left to right, each position conditioned on everything before it. Almost all models people use today — GPT, Claude, Llama — work this way.
Attention mask
A matrix that controls which parts of a sequence each position is allowed to look at when a model is computing its outputs. Causal masking lets only leftward context through (standard in autoregressive models). Bidirectional masking lets everything through (standard in diffusion models). SimSD's trick is constructing a temporary causal-style mask just for the draft tokens.
Export controls
Legal restrictions on the sale or transfer of technology to foreign countries or entities. Traditionally applied to weapons and dual-use hardware like semiconductors. The Fable 5 suspension is one of the first applications of export-control law to a commercial software model.
Throughput
In AI inference, how many tokens (words or word-pieces) the model can generate per second. Higher throughput means faster responses and lower cost to run. The 7.46× figure in the SimSD paper means a task that would have taken 7.46 seconds now takes roughly one.
