The shorthand no one learned

The most powerful publicly available reasoning model yet arrived on Sunday morning, while a six-person team published evidence this week that what language models "read" doesn't need to look like language at all.

The one thing

On Sunday, June 22, Google launched Gemini 2.5 Pro with Deep Think — the company's most capable model ever released to the public. Deep Think is Google's extended-reasoning mode: instead of responding immediately, the model generates multiple candidate solution paths, evaluates each one, and selects the best before composing its final answer. On the hardest public benchmarks — USAMO mathematics, LiveCodeBench, and Humanity's Last Exam — Gemini 2.5 Pro with Deep Think now leads every other publicly available model. Its context window is two million tokens, meaning it can read and reason over roughly 1,500 pages of text in a single session. It is available now through Google AI Studio, the Gemini API, and Vertex AI. The one area where it does not yet lead: software engineering. Anthropic's Fable 5 still holds the top score on SWE-bench and agentic coding tasks — though, as of midnight last night, Fable 5 is no longer free.

Paper, explained: when the text is unreadable — but the meaning survives

"Large Language Models Do Not Always Need Readable Language" · Jiayi Zhu, Haoxuan Peng, Junxi Wang, Liang Ke, Chen Zhang & Linfeng Zhang · arXiv:2606.19857 · Submitted June 18, 2026

Every AI tool you have ever used assumes, at least implicitly, that the text you type is meaningful because it looks like text. This paper quietly removes that assumption — and finds it was not load-bearing.

The problem, and why it's hard

When you send a message to a language model, it does not read the way you do. Your text is first broken into tokens — fragments of words, or whole short words — which are converted to numbers, processed through billions of multiplications, and converted back into numbers that become new text. The words are a delivery vehicle, not the computation itself. But tokens are expensive: every one costs processing time and, via APIs, money. Researchers have long wondered whether you could communicate the same meaning with fewer of them — strip away the human-readable layer and speak more directly to the model. The objection blocking this has always been that language models were trained almost entirely on human text, so they presumably needed human text to function. This paper tests that assumption directly.

How it works

Think of court stenography. Reporters train for years to write in a shorthand that looks like indecipherable scratches to anyone else in the room. Speed matters in court; prose is too slow. Stenography trades legibility for density — each strange mark carries meaning that would otherwise take a full sentence. BabelTele is the same trade-off, with one twist: instead of a human designing the shorthand, the model designs it for itself.

Rather than imposing a fixed compression scheme, the six researchers studied what happens when you ask an instruction-tuned language model to develop its own compact representations. The model receives pairs of (original text, compressed form) and learns to generate and decode more of them. There is no dictionary or rule book — the model discovers whatever notation it can produce and interpret reliably. The resulting representations, in the paper's own description, "substantially depart from ordinary natural language." To a human reader, they look like noise: garbled syllables, odd punctuation, fragments that carry no obvious sense. To the model that generated them, they are perfectly legible.

The key mechanism is instruction-tuning — the training stage where a base model is fine-tuned on (instruction, response) examples to become an assistant. The researchers found this stage is what grants models the flexibility to operate outside normal prose patterns. Base models — trained only to predict the next word, without any instruction fine-tuning — could not reliably recover meaning from BabelTele. The assistants embedded in commercial AI products can.

What they actually did

The team evaluated BabelTele across four independent methods. Readability diagnostics confirmed the representations do not look like language. Model likelihood measures checked whether the model itself considered BabelTele statistically plausible. Human questionnaires established that readers cannot recover the original meaning from the compressed form. And downstream task evaluations — the practical test — measured whether using BabelTele as input changed what the model got right on a battery of tasks. The key number across all four: BabelTele condensed text to 27.9% of its original volume while maintaining 99.5% semantic fidelity. That is roughly a 3.6× compression — the equivalent of replacing a 1,000-word essay with a 279-word representation that no human can decode, but that the model can use as if nothing changed.

What they found

The results held across different types of content — the compression was not tuned for a specific task and does not need to be. Instruction-tuned models significantly outperformed base models, confirming that the capacity to work with non-standard representations emerges during the fine-tuning stage rather than being baked in at the pretraining level. The notable failure mode: BabelTele representations are not portable. What one model family learns to compress and decode may look opaque to a model from a different lineage. There is no universal compact notation — each model converges on its own dialect of the shorthand.

The catch

This is an exploratory paper, not an engineering blueprint. The 99.5% semantic fidelity figure is measured through downstream task performance — a solid proxy, but not a ground truth. The researchers do not address the most obvious implication: if AI systems process inputs that humans cannot audit, meaningful human oversight becomes harder to maintain. That consequence goes unexamined. The paper plants a flag and hands the question — engineering and governance alike — to whoever picks it up next.

If you remember one thing: the legibility of an AI's inputs is a design choice, not a technical requirement — and once you realize that, a lot of quiet assumptions start asking to be questioned.

Why it matters

Google's Deep Think launch and the BabelTele paper are, on the surface, unrelated. But they point in the same direction: the frontier of what AI can do is increasingly happening in formats that the human in the loop cannot directly read. Deep Think explores solution paths internally before surfacing one answer. BabelTele processes meaning in a form no human can follow. Boris Cherny's loops run thousands of agents without a person prompting each one. In each case, the interface between the person and the AI is one step further removed from the actual work.

This is largely a benefit — more capability, lower cost, less friction. But it is worth being precise about the trade-off: the efficiency gains come partly from removing the human-readable layer. That layer has always been a form of transparency, and of accountability. When it goes from optional to absent, oversight becomes a design question rather than a default. No single development here is alarming; the direction is worth watching.

Decoder

Word of the day
Semantic fidelity
How well the meaning of a piece of text is preserved when you change its surface form. A perfect translation carries 100% semantic fidelity — different words, same meaning. BabelTele achieves 99.5% fidelity while looking nothing like the original. "Semantic" means to do with meaning, as opposed to "syntactic" (to do with structure and grammar).
Token
The smallest unit of text a language model reads — roughly three-quarters of a word in English. "Strawberry" is one token; "extraordinarily" is three or four. Every token costs processing time and API money. Fewer tokens is the entire efficiency game.
Instruction-tuning
A training stage where a base model — trained only to predict the next word — is further trained on (instruction, response) pairs to become a useful assistant. BabelTele works for instruction-tuned models but not for base models; the fine-tuning stage seems to be what teaches flexibility with non-standard text.
Deep Think
Google's name for Gemini 2.5 Pro's extended-reasoning mode. Instead of answering immediately, the model generates multiple candidate solution paths, evaluates them, then picks one before responding. Slower than a direct answer, but measurably more accurate on hard science and maths problems.
MoE (Mixture of Experts)
An architecture where a large model is divided into specialist sub-networks called experts, with only a few active for any given input. DeepSeek V4-Pro has 1.6 trillion parameters but activates only 49 billion per query — a big model at a fraction of the running cost.
Context window
The total amount of text a model can read and reason over at once, measured in tokens. Gemini 2.5 Pro's 2 million tokens is roughly 1,500 pages — a career's worth of meeting notes, in a single session.
