The practice and the performance

OpenAI launches three new models this morning — each aimed at a different point on the cost-capability curve — while the week's most-read research paper uncovers a quiet flaw baked into how every modern reasoning model is trained: the version the training engine polishes may not be the version the user actually talks to.

Paper, explained: the model you train isn't always the model you ship

The Mirage of Optimizing Training Policies: Monotonic Inference Policies as the Real Objective for LLM Reinforcement Learning · Hongyao Tang et al. · arXiv 2606.29526

This paper ranked #1 on Hugging Face's trending papers list this week — and its central claim is quietly unsettling. Every reinforcement-learning step taken to improve a language model may be improving a version of that model the user never actually encounters.

The problem, and why it's hard

For the past few years, the standard method for making language models smarter has been reinforcement learning: give the model feedback on its answers (good answer earns a reward, bad answer earns a penalty) so it gradually learns to do better. Systems like ChatGPT and every major reasoning model use some variant of this — named RLHF, GRPO, PPO, and so on.

Here's what's hiding under the hood. Training a large model is extremely expensive, so engineers split the job into two separate systems: a training engine that computes gradients and updates the model's parameters, and an inference engine that runs the model fast to generate text. For speed, the inference engine during training often stores numbers in a compressed format called FP8 (8-bit floating point). The deployed model, serving real users, typically runs in a higher-precision format — BF16 or FP16. Same weights, different number formats, subtly different outputs for the same input.

This discrepancy is called the training–inference mismatch. Until this paper, it was treated mostly as an engineering inconvenience. The authors argue it's something more serious: a structural flaw in how the learning objective is framed. When RL updates improve the training-engine version of the model, there is no mathematical guarantee that the inference-engine version also improves. You are optimizing one thing, but shipping another.

How it works

Think of it like rehearsing a speech. You practice in a small, quiet studio — the training engine — with a coach who grades every line. You get very good at studio speaking. But when you step onto the actual stage — the inference engine, which has different acoustics, slight ambient noise, different dynamics — some of what you rehearsed carries over, and some doesn't. Your coach's feedback optimized the studio version of your performance. The audience is in the theatre.

The authors formalize this precisely. In RL for LLMs, each update shifts the training engine's policy — the probability it assigns to every possible next word — in a direction meant to earn more reward. After the update, weights are synchronized over to the inference engine. But because of the precision gap, the inference engine now assigns slightly different probabilities to the same sequences. "Slightly different" can compound across a long generation: the inference policy may have drifted sideways or even backward, even as the training policy stepped forward.

Their proposed fix is called MIPU — Monotonic Inference Policy Update. "Monotonic" means only-ever-improving: every accepted update should provably improve the inference policy, never regress it. MIPU works in two steps. First, generate a candidate update on the training side, as normal. Second, before applying it, check whether the update also benefits the inference engine, using a lightweight proxy measurement called an "inference-side gap." If the gap shows the inference policy would regress, the update is rejected. Only updates that pass both tests — improving training and inference — get applied to the weights.

What they actually did

The researchers tested MIPU on two scales of the Qwen3 model family: Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-1.7B (where the suffix is the parameter count — 4 billion and 1.7 billion numbers in the model, respectively). They deliberately used FP8-quantized rollout — the highest-mismatch setting, where training and inference engines disagree the most. MIPU was compared against standard RL baselines, measuring both reasoning accuracy across math and coding benchmarks and training stability (whether performance scores swing erratically between update steps).

What they found

Across both model scales, MIPU achieved the best average reasoning performance and notably more stable training dynamics than the standard baselines. The instability typical of RL training — where benchmark scores can jump and crash between steps — was substantially reduced. The authors interpret this as direct evidence that the training–inference mismatch is not merely theoretical: it produces measurable, harmful noise in standard pipelines, and fixing it pays off in practice.

The catch

The experiment targets one specific source of mismatch — FP8 quantization during rollout — not all the ways training and inference can diverge (different batch sizes, attention approximations, kernel implementations). The "inference-side gap proxy" is also an approximation; it can't perfectly measure the true inference improvement. And the models tested are relatively small. Whether MIPU scales cleanly to the 70B or 400B parameter range used by frontier systems is an open question.

If you remember one thing: Every RL-trained model in production may have been subtly optimizing a ghost of itself — and this paper is the first rigorous attempt to close that gap.

Why it matters

Two things this week sit at opposite ends of the same story. One: OpenAI's GPT-5.6 shows how quickly frontier capability is being packaged, priced, and cleared for global deployment — three tiers, record inference speeds, same-day government sign-off. Two: a paper from inside RL research reveals that the foundations of that capability may carry a quiet structural flaw that has been present in every major reasoning model for years. These aren't contradictions. They're two views of the same machine. Capability is compounding faster than the understanding of it — and the fact that someone publishing on arXiv is still finding basic miswirings in the training loop is a useful reminder that the engine running the world's most capable systems is still, in important ways, a work in progress.

Decoder

Word of the day
Quantization
Compressing a model's numbers into a smaller, less precise format — think rounding every measurement from millimetres to centimetres. It makes a model run faster and cheaper, but introduces tiny errors. The gap between how a model is trained and how it runs in deployment often comes down to different levels of this rounding.
Reinforcement learning (RL)
Training a model by rewarding good outputs and penalizing bad ones — the same basic idea as training a dog, scaled to billions of numbers.
Training–inference mismatch
When the version of a model that receives RL training updates runs under different conditions than the version that answers real users, causing the two to behave slightly differently from the same weights.
FP8 / BF16
Number formats of different precision. FP8 uses 8 bits per number (fast, less accurate); BF16 uses 16 bits (slower, more accurate). Training engines often use FP8; deployed models tend to use BF16.
Policy
In RL, the model's learned strategy for choosing what to say — expressed as a probability over every possible next word. Optimizing the policy means shifting those probabilities toward better answers.
Ultra mode
GPT-5.6 Sol's new setting that spawns multiple parallel subagents to work on different parts of a complex task simultaneously, trading cost for speed and depth on hard problems.
