Twenty-eight million questions

The week an AI lab discovered that a fleet of ghost accounts had been systematically interrogating its best model for two months — and using every answer to build a competitor.

The one thing

Anthropic has written to US senators Elizabeth Warren and Tim Scott, and separately to the White House, accusing operators linked to Alibaba's AI unit of running what it calls "the largest known distillation attack" it has ever faced. Between April 22 and June 5, 2026, nearly 25,000 fraudulent accounts generated 28.8 million exchanges with Claude — not to get answers they needed, but to collect answers in bulk. The goal, Anthropic alleges, was distillation: using Claude's outputs as training data to teach a rival model — likely in Alibaba's Qwen AI lab — to reason and code the way Claude does, without ever seeing Claude's underlying code or weights. The attack was specifically targeted at Claude's software-engineering and agentic-reasoning capabilities, the company says. Alibaba has not publicly responded.

Paper, explained: how model theft actually works

A Survey on Model Extraction Attacks and Defenses for Large Language Models · Zhao, Li, Ding, Gong, Zhao, Dong · KDD 2026 · arXiv 2506.22521

A survey published this week at KDD 2026 — one of the top data-mining conferences — provides the clearest academic framework yet for understanding exactly what Alibaba is alleged to have done to Claude, and why it works, and why stopping it is so hard.

The problem, and why it's hard

When a company deploys an AI model as an API — a service anyone can query over the internet for a fee — it faces an unusual kind of vulnerability. Unlike a physical factory, which you can fence off and guard, an API is designed to be used by strangers. You can't tell, from the outside, whether someone asking a question wants the answer or wants to study how you answered it. And if someone asks enough questions and collects all the answers, they have something extraordinarily valuable: a dataset of how a world-class AI thinks, which they can use to teach their own model to think the same way.

This is not a theoretical risk. It's called a model extraction attack, and it's been understood in theory for years. What's changed is scale. A survey of the problem until now would have described isolated experiments on simple image classifiers. This paper is the first comprehensive taxonomy of extraction attacks specifically against large language models — systems like Claude, GPT, and Gemini — where the stakes are much higher and the attack surface is much larger.

How it works

The best everyday analogy is a restaurant. Imagine a culinary genius whose restaurant has a years-long waiting list. You can't get the recipe — it's proprietary. But you can order dishes. If you send enough people to that restaurant, order every combination on the menu, taste everything, and write down precise descriptions of every dish, you begin to understand how the kitchen works. After enough visits, a skilled chef reading your notes could recreate most of the restaurant's output without ever setting foot inside it. You never stole the recipe — you read the food.

Model extraction works the same way. The paper identifies three distinct flavours of attack:

Functionality extraction is the restaurant-menu approach. You send thousands or millions of inputs — code problems, reasoning puzzles, writing tasks — collect all the outputs, and use those input-output pairs as training data for your own model. The more targeted your questions, the better the copy. This is what Anthropic alleges Alibaba did: 28.8 million conversations, chosen specifically to elicit Claude's software-engineering and agentic-reasoning capabilities. The copy may not be identical to the original, but it learns the same moves.

Training data extraction goes deeper. Certain kinds of prompts can cause a language model to regurgitate text it memorised during training — phone numbers, passages from books, source code, private conversations that leaked into training data. The model doesn't know it's doing something wrong; it's just completing the pattern. The attacker is effectively reaching through the model into the original training dataset.

Prompt stealing targets something different: the hidden instructions companies place at the start of a conversation to customise the model's behaviour. Every commercial AI deployment has a system prompt — rules about what to answer, how to answer it, what to decline. Certain adversarial techniques can extract those instructions, letting a competitor duplicate not just the model's capabilities but its specific configuration and persona.

What they actually did

The authors reviewed hundreds of papers across all three attack categories and produced a unified taxonomy — the first to cover the full landscape for large language models specifically. They also designed new evaluation metrics, because the old ones, developed for simple classification models, don't translate well to generative systems. Measuring how "similar" two image classifiers are is straightforward; measuring how "similar" two text generators are, across open-ended tasks, is genuinely hard. The paper proposes ways to assess extraction fidelity and defense effectiveness that work for the generative setting.

What they found

The survey's most sobering finding is that defenses have not kept up with attacks. The strategies that work reasonably well against simpler models — adding noise to outputs, reducing confidence scores, throttling the API — all hurt model quality before they actually stop a determined extractor. Someone running 28.8 million queries across 25,000 accounts, as Alibaba is alleged to have done, can afford to average over a lot of noise.

The most promising direction the paper identifies is watermarking: embedding a subtle statistical signature in how the model writes — invisible to readers, unremovable in normal use — that survives the training process and can later prove a suspicious model was distilled from a specific source. If Anthropic's outputs carry a watermark, and Alibaba's Qwen model carries the same watermark, that's evidence. It doesn't prevent the theft, but it creates a paper trail. The catch is that strong watermarks are hard to design, and skilled attackers can sometimes scrub them.

The catch

This is a survey, not a solution. The authors map the terrain honestly but don't claim to have solved it. Much of the defense research they review was evaluated on older, smaller models, and results at the scale of frontier systems — where the attacks are most dangerous — are sparse. The hard truth the paper arrives at is that the most capable distillation attacks remain very difficult to fully prevent without meaningfully degrading the product. An API that's hard enough to query in bulk is also, in some ways, hard enough to use.

If you remember one thing: Distillation lets someone approximate a billion-dollar AI model using only its public outputs — and we don't yet have a defense that stops this without hurting the model.

Why it matters

Two threads run through this week. One is the infrastructure race: OpenAI building its own chip, Micron printing its best-ever earnings on AI memory demand, Qualcomm pursuing a $10 billion acquisition to compete in accelerators. Every company that matters in AI wants to own more of the physical stack — more of the silicon, more of the data centers, more of the supply chain. The dependence on Nvidia is being treated as a strategic liability across the board.

The other thread is a property question that doesn't have a clean answer. If the only thing separating a competitor from your AI model's capabilities is the terms of service that users accept before querying it, and a determined adversary can route around those terms through 25,000 accounts, then what does it mean to own an AI model? The distillation attack on Claude isn't just an IP dispute — it's a demonstration that the standard toolkit for protecting intellectual property doesn't map cleanly onto a product that works by answering questions. You can patent the architecture. You can copyright the training data. But you cannot copyright an answer.

Decoder

Word of the day
Distillation attack
Using an AI model's own outputs as training data to teach a cheaper copy to behave the same way — without seeing the original model's code or weights. Like sending a hundred food critics to a restaurant until you've reconstructed the recipes from their tasting notes.
Model extraction
The broader family of attacks that includes distillation. Any technique for duplicating an AI model's capabilities by studying what it says rather than how it was built.
ASIC
Application-Specific Integrated Circuit. A chip designed to do one thing very well — in Jalapeño's case, running large language models — rather than the general-purpose chips (like Intel or AMD processors) found in ordinary computers.
HBM4
High Bandwidth Memory, 4th generation. The extremely fast memory standard used inside AI accelerator chips. Crucial because AI models need to move vast amounts of data in and out of memory many times per second during inference.
Watermarking
Embedding invisible statistical patterns in an AI model's outputs — patterns that survive if someone trains a new model on those outputs. Lets the original owner later prove a suspicious competitor model was distilled from theirs.
Inference
The process of running a trained AI model to answer a question or generate text. Distinct from training (which is done once, at enormous cost); inference happens millions of times a day and is what chips like Jalapeño are optimised for.
