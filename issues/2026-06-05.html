Before the states could disagree

A bipartisan bill lands in Congress to freeze fifty different state AI rules into one federal standard — the same week a new technique quietly gives AI models a better working memory. Both stories are really about what happens when a system gets bigger and something has to give.

The one thing

On Thursday, six lawmakers — three Democrats, three Republicans — unveiled a 269-page discussion draft called The Great American Artificial Intelligence Act. The bill's central clause would override any state law "specifically regulating the development" of an AI model for three years. That means safety and transparency requirements recently passed in California and New York would be paused at the federal level. The draft was expected for months and is still far from becoming law, but it is the clearest signal yet that Congress wants to own the question of what AI companies must do before they ship — and it drew immediate opposition from safety advocates, who called federal preemption of state rules a "generational mistake."

Paper, explained: giving AI a better working memory

KVarN: Variance-Normalized KV-Cache Quantization Mitigates Error Accumulation in Reasoning Tasks · Huawei Computing Systems Lab · arxiv.org/abs/2606.03458

As AI models get better at "thinking longer" before they answer — reconsidering, checking, refining — they hit a quiet wall: memory. A new paper from Huawei's computing lab cracks open that wall without sacrificing accuracy, and the method is clean enough to drop straight into production with a single line of configuration.

The problem, and why it's hard

Every time a language model generates text, it keeps a running record of the conversation so far. This record is called the KV-cache — short for "key-value cache" — and it acts like the model's working notepad. Each new word the model writes is influenced by everything on that notepad, so the notepad grows continuously. For a short exchange this is fine. But one of the most effective techniques in modern AI is test-time scaling: letting the model spend more time reasoning before it commits to an answer, which means much longer sequences, which means a much fatter notepad.

GPU memory is finite. Engineers deal with this through quantization — compressing the notepad by storing numbers in a coarser form. Instead of writing 3.14159265, you write 3.14. The problem is that during autoregressive decoding — the process of generating one word at a time, each word depending on all prior ones — those rounding errors don't stay put. They compound. Each new token is generated slightly wrong, which nudges the next one slightly wronger, in a chain that drifts further from full precision the longer the model reasons. Prior benchmarks missed this because they measured compression quality in "prefill mode" (processing a fixed input all at once), not in generation mode (producing output token by token). The error profile is different, and it turns out much worse for reasoning tasks.

How it works

The authors traced the compounding error to a single cause: the values inside the KV-cache have wildly unequal "loudness" across channels. Some dimensions of the cache hold very large numbers; others hold tiny ones. Any rounding scheme that treats every channel the same will mangle the loud channels badly and waste precision on the quiet ones.

Imagine a sound engineer trying to record a band where the drummer plays at 100 decibels and the triangle player at 40. If you set the recording level for the average, you clip the drums into distortion and lose the triangle entirely. The trick is to give each instrument its own level before mixing down.

KVarN does this in two steps. First, it applies a Hadamard rotation — a well-known mathematical transformation that takes all the "loudness" in the cache and spreads it evenly across every channel, eliminating the outliers. Think of it as scrambling the band's seats so no single player dominates any one microphone. Second, it applies dual-scaling variance normalization, equalizing the remaining variance across both dimensions of the key and value matrices at once — rows and columns simultaneously — using an iterative method borrowed from optimal-transport mathematics (the Sinkhorn algorithm, used elsewhere to solve shipping and assignment problems). At read time, a folded scale factor undoes the normalization with almost no extra computation. The whole pipeline is calibration-free: no warm-up batch of examples needed. You flip a flag in vLLM — the most widely used open-source AI inference server — and it is on.

What they actually did

The team tested on Qwen3-32B, a capable 32-billion-parameter open model, against AIME25 math competition problems (long-horizon reasoning requiring 16,000-token contexts) and HumanEval coding challenges. They used 4-bit compression for key matrices and an aggressive 2-bit compression for value matrices — the tighter the compression, the harder the challenge. They compared against FP16 (uncompressed full precision) and against TurboQuant, the previous state-of-the-art KV-cache quantization method.

What they found

On AIME25 at 16K context, KVarN matches FP16 accuracy — meaning full-precision output quality at a fraction of the memory cost. It delivers 3–5× more KV-cache capacity than uncompressed FP16, and up to ~1.3× the throughput. Against TurboQuant it achieves ~2.4× the throughput at higher accuracy. The improvements held on HumanEval coding problems as well as math, suggesting the method generalizes beyond one task type.

The catch

All the headline results are on one model family (Qwen3-32B) and two benchmarks. The 2-bit value quantization is aggressive — it works on math and code, which have regular, structured outputs, but it has not been rigorously tested on more open-ended generation (storytelling, multi-turn dialogue) where error patterns may differ. The paper comes from an industry lab with obvious incentives to ship; independent replication on other model families would strengthen the claims. It is also worth noting that "calibration-free" does not mean zero overhead — the Hadamard rotation and Sinkhorn passes add a small upfront cost.

If you remember one thing: Compressing an AI's working memory used to cause errors to snowball over long conversations; KVarN shows you can halve the memory cost with near-zero accuracy loss by equalizing each memory channel's "loudness" before you round it down.

Why it matters

The week's two biggest stories cut in opposite directions, but they share a structure. In Washington, Congress wants to consolidate — replace fifty state-level experiments with one federal standard, at least for three years, on the theory that too many different rules make a coherent national industry impossible. In the lab, the KVarN paper's entire contribution is the opposite move: spread the load out, equalize the variance, stop any one channel from monopolizing the error budget. Both are responses to the same underlying pressure — a system that grew faster than its original architecture could handle. In regulation, as in engineering, the answer is the same: if you don't normalize, everything piles up in one place and something breaks.

The practical question for the bill is whether a three-year pause on state safety laws is a calibration step or a capitulation. Safety groups say the strongest state laws are the only real guardrails in place right now. Industry argues that fifty inconsistent standards make it impossible to ship anything responsibly at scale. Both are making a quantization argument: how much precision can you afford to lose before the answer is simply wrong?

Word of the day
Preemption
In US law, when a higher level of government passes a rule that "pre-empts" — overrides — a lower level's rule. Like a building fire code from the state marshal that overrides a local landlord's lease requirements. The House AI bill would preempt state laws the same way: federal standard wins, state rule paused.
KV-cache
The running memory an AI keeps of everything said in a conversation. Stands for "key-value cache." The longer the exchange, the larger the cache — and the more GPU memory it consumes.
Quantization
Storing numbers in a compressed, coarser form to save memory and speed up computation. Like writing "3.14" instead of "3.14159265." Saves space, but introduces small rounding errors.
Test-time scaling
Letting an AI spend more computation at answer time — thinking longer, re-checking — to produce better results. Works well but makes the KV-cache balloon.
Hadamard rotation
A mathematical transformation that spreads information evenly across all dimensions of a matrix. Named after French mathematician Jacques Hadamard. Used here to eliminate outlier values before compression.
Autoregressive decoding
The way language models generate text: one word (token) at a time, each new word depending on all previous ones. This is why errors in the working memory accumulate — each step builds on the last.
