Twenty-Nine Founding Members

China's biggest annual technology gathering closed this weekend with something new: a formal international body for AI cooperation, backed by 29 countries and headquartered in Shanghai. The same week produced the world's largest open-weight AI model and a quietly surprising paper — a small academic team showed that a four-billion-parameter model, engineered thoughtfully, can outperform systems with hundreds of times more computing power at understanding what's happening in a video.

The one thing

On July 16, representatives from 29 countries — including Russia, Indonesia, Kazakhstan, Pakistan, and Laos — signed an agreement in Shanghai establishing the World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization, or WAICO. It is designed as an independent intergovernmental body, open to all sovereign states, with a focus on development and what its founders call "equitable" global AI governance. UN Secretary-General António Guterres attended the signing ceremony. Chinese President Xi Jinping delivered the opening keynote at the World AI Conference the following morning, the first time he has addressed the event directly. Xi called for an international framework that gives every country — not just the wealthiest — meaningful access to AI's benefits and a voice in setting its rules. Whether WAICO develops real authority is an open question: it has no enforcement mechanism yet, and the countries that dominate frontier AI development — the United States and its closest allies — are conspicuously absent from its founding roster. But 29 governments signed their names to it, and the UN Secretary-General showed up. That is not nothing.

Paper, explained: the small model that could see

"VideoChat3: Fully Open Video MLLM for Efficient and Generalist Video Understanding" · 27 researchers from Nanjing University, Shanghai AI Laboratory, Nanyang Technological University, Peking University · arXiv 2607.14935

A team of 27 researchers released a video-understanding model with 4 billion parameters — a size considered modest at a moment when frontier systems run to the hundreds of billions — and proceeded to outscore GPT-5 and Gemini 2.5 Flash on the benchmarks that specifically test whether a model knows when something happens in a video. The paper was posted July 16, and it reached the top tier of Hugging Face's trending papers within a day.

The problem, and why it's hard

Most people interact with video as a passive medium — you watch it, you feel it. But building a computer system that can watch a video and answer precise questions about timing is surprisingly hard. The challenge has a name: temporal grounding. A system has to be able to answer questions like "at exactly what moment does the teacher pick up the pen?" or "find the 12-second clip where the car turns left." That's not the same as recognizing that a pen, a teacher, and a car are present. It requires tracking change across time.

Current large video models handle this poorly for a structural reason. They were designed around image-understanding — each frame is analyzed like a still photograph, and the frames are stitched together. That works tolerably for questions about content ("is there a dog in this video?") but fails for motion questions, because the change between frames is never directly computed. The model sees a slideshow; it doesn't see flow.

A second problem is cost. Processing video at high resolution is brutally expensive: a standard video understanding model might generate thousands of "visual tokens" for a single clip — each one consuming compute. Scaling that to long videos, or to real-time streaming, becomes impractical quickly.

How it works — the analogy

Imagine trying to understand a dance performance. You could describe it by writing down what each dancer looks like in a series of photographs, taken one per second. Or you could describe the movement itself — the arc of an arm, the direction of a step. The second description captures something the first misses entirely, and it takes fewer words.

VideoChat3's core innovation, the Inflated 3D Vision Transformer (I3D-ViT), works like the second approach. A standard vision transformer — the type of model that reads images — processes 2D patches of pixels. The team "inflated" those 2D reading patterns into 3D ones, so the model explicitly computes relationships between patches across neighboring frames, not just within a single frame. It doesn't just see what's in each image; it sees how things are moving between images.

The second piece is Adaptive Frame Resolution: rather than sampling every frame at the same resolution and cost, the model adjusts on the fly. Fast-moving action gets more tokens; static scenes get fewer. The result is that VideoChat3 generates roughly half the visual tokens that comparable models like Qwen3-VL produce for the same video — meaning it costs less to run, can process longer videos, and is practical for live streaming, not just pre-recorded clips.

The team also built three custom training datasets totalling approximately 3 million instruction samples, covering general video understanding, long-form content, and streaming scenarios. All of it — the model weights, training code, and datasets — was released publicly alongside the paper.

What they actually did

The researchers evaluated VideoChat3 on the TimeLens benchmark suite, which tests temporal grounding across three standard tasks: Charades-STA (fine-grained activity localization in short clips), ActivityNet Captions (finding moments described in natural language in longer videos), and QVHighlights (highlight detection and moment retrieval). They ran the same benchmarks on GPT-5 and Gemini 2.5 Flash for direct comparison. The metric is mIoU — mean Intersection over Union — which measures how closely the model's predicted time window overlaps with the ground-truth window. Higher is better; a score of 100 would mean a perfect match every time.

What they found

VideoChat3 (4B parameters) beat both GPT-5 and Gemini 2.5 Flash on all three tasks:

  • Charades-STA: VideoChat3 scored 56.1 mIoU, versus GPT-5's 40.5 and Gemini 2.5 Flash's 48.6.
  • ActivityNet Captions: 54.6 versus 42.9 (GPT-5) and 52.5 (Gemini).
  • QVHighlights: 67.0 versus 52.1 (GPT-5) and 64.3 (Gemini).

These aren't marginal differences. On Charades-STA, VideoChat3's score is nearly 40% higher than GPT-5's. The pattern holds across all three tasks: a model a fraction of the size, built by a university lab, systematically outperforms the best proprietary commercial systems on this particular capability. The efficiency gains are similarly striking — half the visual token cost for comparable or better results.

The catch

These benchmarks specifically test temporal grounding, which is one slice of what a video-understanding model needs to do. On broader video understanding tasks — general Q&A, summarization, multi-modal reasoning — the largest frontier models still hold substantial advantages. VideoChat3 is not a general replacement for GPT-5 at video; it's a specialist that has been deliberately optimized for the temporal understanding component. The training datasets, while large, were curated by the research team and may reflect the specific task formats of the benchmarks they're targeting. How well the efficiency claims hold up on domain-shifted real-world video (sports, medical imaging, live events) is untested.

If you remember one thing: a 4-billion-parameter academic model just outscored GPT-5 at knowing when things happen in videos — by teaching itself to track motion between frames rather than reading each frame like a photograph.

Why it matters

Three things happened this week that individually seem unrelated but together sketch the same picture. A 29-nation body formed in Shanghai to claim a seat at the table in AI governance — one conspicuously free of the countries building the most powerful systems. A 4-billion-parameter open model outperformed the leading proprietary giants on a specific, measurable task. And the world's largest open-weight model arrived from a Chinese lab, free to download. The common thread: the assumption that frontier AI is a small club controlled by a handful of Western companies is under real pressure, from multiple directions at once. Access, influence, and raw capability are all becoming more distributed. That doesn't mean the playing field is equal — the gaps remain enormous, and WAICO has no enforcement power yet. But the shape of the landscape is changing faster than the governance frameworks designed to manage it.

Decoder

Word of the day
Temporal grounding
The ability to pinpoint when something happens in a video, not just whether it happens. If you ask "find the moment the teacher drops the pen," a model doing temporal grounding returns a time window, not a yes or no. It's the difference between a witness who saw the event and one who can tell you the exact minute it occurred.
MLLM
Multimodal Large Language Model — a language model that can also process images, video, or audio, not just text. VideoChat3 is a video MLLM: it takes video as input alongside text questions.
mIoU
Mean Intersection over Union. A score from 0 to 100 that measures how much a predicted time window overlaps with the correct answer. A score of 67 means the model's window overlaps about two-thirds of the real answer on average. Higher is better.
MoE (Mixture of Experts)
A model architecture where only a fraction of the network is "switched on" for any given input. Kimi K3's 2.8 trillion total parameters sounds enormous, but for each request, only a small active subset processes the tokens. It lets a model be big without paying the full cost on every query.
Open-weight
A model whose numerical parameters (the "weights" that encode what it has learned) are publicly released for anyone to download. Open-weight models can be run locally, modified, and deployed without the lab's permission or servers — unlike commercial APIs where you send data to someone else's system.
I3D-ViT
Inflated 3D Vision Transformer. VideoChat3's core innovation: a standard image-reading pattern stretched into three dimensions so the model computes how pixels relate across neighboring frames, not just within a single frame. It "sees" motion directly rather than inferring it from a slideshow of stills.
